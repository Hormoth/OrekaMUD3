# DYBBUK

The goblin stops mid-sentence. Its companions notice the silence first — Krikkit never stops talking, has not stopped talking since the day it learned Common from a halfling trader — and then they notice the eyes. Krikkit's eyes have gone flat. Not dead. Not vacant. Full, the way a cup is full when someone has poured something different into it. The goblin's mouth opens and a voice comes out that is not Krikkit's voice. It is older, heavier, and it speaks Goblin with an accent that hasn't been used in three hundred years.

### Combat

A dybbuk does not fight in its incorporeal form if it can avoid it. It is a possessor, not a combatant — its goal is to enter a living goblinoid body and use it to complete whatever task anchors it to the world. In incorporeal form, it is vulnerable: low hit points, no damage reduction, and a touch attack that deals Wisdom damage but does not kill quickly.

When forced to fight without a host, a dybbuk uses its wail of the unfinished to stun nearby creatures, then attempts to possess the most vulnerable goblinoid in range. If no goblinoid is available, it flees through the nearest wall or floor, searching for a goblinoid community to infiltrate.

When possessing a host, a dybbuk fights with whatever the host's body is capable of — the dybbuk retains the host's physical statistics, equipment, and proficiencies, overlaid with the dybbuk's mental ability scores, skills, and purpose-driven aggression. A possessed goblin warrior is a dangerous opponent because it fights without fear, without pain, and without the self-preservation instinct that normally limits goblinoid aggression.

A dybbuk does not willingly abandon a host. It must be forced out through magic (protection from evil, dispel evil) or through the host's death. If the host dies, the dybbuk is ejected in incorporeal form and is stunned for 1 round, during which it is vulnerable.

### Special Abilities

**Wisdom Damage (Su):** A dybbuk's incorporeal touch deals 1d4 Wisdom damage. The save DC to resist is 17 (Will negates). The damage manifests as confusion, disorientation, and the sensation of hearing a voice inside your skull that is not yours. The save DC is Charisma-based. A creature reduced to 0 Wisdom is unconscious and vulnerable to possession.

**Possession (Su):** As a full-round action, a dybbuk can attempt to possess a goblinoid creature within 5 feet. The target must succeed on a Will save (DC 19) or be possessed. The dybbuk enters the target's body and takes control, suppressing the host's personality and memories (but not erasing them — the host is aware of everything that happens while possessed, but cannot act). The save DC is Charisma-based and includes the +2 from Ability Focus.

A dybbuk can only possess goblinoid creatures — goblins, hobgoblins, bugbears, and almas. It cannot possess Kin, animals, or non-goblinoid humanoids. This limitation is absolute and defining: the dybbuk is a goblinoid spirit, and its possession resonates only with goblinoid physiology.

The possession lasts until the host body dies, the dybbuk is forced out by magic (protection from evil suppresses the possession for the spell's duration; dispel evil, DC 19, ejects the dybbuk permanently), or the dybbuk voluntarily departs. A possessed creature radiates a faint aura of undeath detectable with detect undead.

**Wail of the Unfinished (Su):** Once per day, a dybbuk can release a keening wail — a sound of desperate, frustrated incompletion — that affects all creatures within 30 feet. Targets must succeed on a Will save (DC 17) or be stunned for 1 round. This is a sonic, mind-affecting effect. The save DC is Charisma-based. The wail sounds different to every listener: some hear a scream of rage, others a sob of grief, others a single word repeated in a language they don't speak.

**Goblinoid Resonance (Su):** On Kin-sense, a dybbuk in incorporeal form registers as wild static — the same unreadable signal produced by all goblinoid creatures — but cold, thin, and unmistakably dead. It is wild static heard through ice. A DC 16 Kin-sense check detects the dybbuk; a DC 20 check identifies it as undead rather than living goblinoid.

A dybbuk possessing a host suppresses most of the undead quality — the host reads as a normal goblinoid on casual Kin-sense, with only a faint chill in the static that might be mistaken for the host being unwell. A DC 22 Kin-sense check detects the undead presence beneath the living signal.

**Unfinished Purpose (Su):** Every dybbuk is anchored to the living world by a specific, unfulfilled task — something the goblinoid failed to complete before death. The task varies: protect a specific individual, deliver a message, find a lost object, warn a community of danger, avenge a betrayal. The dybbuk is compelled to pursue this task above all else. If the task is completed, the dybbuk is released — it simply fades, the incorporeal form dissolving into nothing as the purpose that held it unravels.

A creature that discovers a dybbuk's unfinished purpose (through Sense Motive DC 20 against a possessed host, or through commune/speak with dead targeting the dybbuk directly) can potentially resolve the possession peacefully by helping the dybbuk complete its task. This is the preferred resolution in goblin communities that understand what a dybbuk is.

### Society

Dybbuk are the restless dead of the goblinoid peoples — spirits of goblins, hobgoblins, bugbears, and almas who died with a critical purpose unfulfilled. They are unique to goblinoid dead; no Kin race produces dybbuk, and no other non-Kin people generates them. The phenomenon is poorly understood by Kin scholars because Kin scholars have limited access to goblinoid death-customs, and the goblinoid peoples themselves treat dybbuk as a private matter — a problem within the community, to be handled by the community.

Free goblin settlements in the Deepwater Marches have the most developed tradition around dybbuk. Goblin shamans (typically adepts or low-level sorcerers) serve as *ruah-speakers* — mediators between the dybbuk and the living community. When a possession is identified, the ruah-speaker's first task is to determine the dybbuk's unfinished purpose through dialogue with the possessed host. If the purpose is achievable (deliver a message, return a stolen item, warn of a specific danger), the community helps complete it, and the dybbuk departs peacefully.

If the purpose is unachievable (avenge a death that occurred centuries ago, find an object that no longer exists, protect a person who is already dead), the ruah-speaker must negotiate — convincing the dybbuk that its purpose cannot be fulfilled and that continuing to possess a living host serves nothing. This negotiation is the hardest part of a ruah-speaker's work, because a dybbuk whose purpose is unachievable is a dybbuk that will never willingly leave, and forcing it out through violence or magic condemns it to wander in incorporeal form until it finds another host.

Warg communities that harbour goblins encounter dybbuk occasionally and treat them with pragmatic tolerance. The Wargs cannot be possessed (they are not goblinoid) but their goblin dependents can, and Warg pack-leaders who discover a possession typically seek out a goblin ruah-speaker to resolve it. Some Warg oral traditions include stories of dybbuk that completed their tasks and departed peacefully — these stories are told with respect, not horror, suggesting that the Wargs view dybbuk as a natural (if inconvenient) part of goblinoid existence.

Dómnathar-aligned goblin auxiliaries produce dybbuk at a higher rate than free goblin populations — the combination of violent death, suppressed autonomy, and unfulfilled commands creates ideal conditions for dybbuk formation. Dómnathar handlers consider dybbuk a nuisance and destroy them when detected, which goblin auxiliaries resent deeply and in silence.

Kin scholars who are aware of dybbuk classify them as "purpose-bound undead" — spirits anchored by task rather than by location or emotion. The Al Karisi is bound by grief; the banshee by grief and location; the dybbuk by purpose alone. This makes dybbuk the most mobile and least predictable of Oreka's undead — they go wherever their purpose leads, crossing regional boundaries and cultural lines without regard for the living world's geography.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (religion) can learn the following about dybbuk.

**DC 15:** Dybbuk are incorporeal undead spirits that possess goblinoid creatures. They can only enter goblins, hobgoblins, bugbears, and almas. A possessed host behaves strangely — speaking in archaic dialects, pursuing unknown goals, fighting without fear.

**DC 20:** Every dybbuk is driven by an unfulfilled purpose from its life. Identifying and completing that purpose causes the dybbuk to depart peacefully. Protection from evil suppresses the possession; dispel evil ejects it permanently. Free goblin communities have ruah-speakers who mediate between dybbuk and the living.

**DC 25:** Dybbuk are unique to goblinoid dead — no other race in Oreka produces them. Dómnathar-aligned goblin populations generate dybbuk at higher rates due to violent deaths and suppressed autonomy. A dybbuk possessing a host is nearly undetectable on Kin-sense (DC 22) — it reads as a living goblinoid with a faint chill. Warg communities treat dybbuk with pragmatic tolerance rather than horror.

# DIRE ANIMALS

In the wild places of Oreka — the deep forests, the far steppe, the high mountains — there are animals that are more than animals. They are larger, stronger, fiercer, and older than anything in their species has a right to be. Their eyes carry a weight of awareness that is not quite intelligence but is more than instinct. Mytroan herders call them *büyük kan* — "great blood." Hasura druids call them *aranduin* — "the sire-marked." Pekakarlik naturalists call them what they are: the offspring of Paragons.

## The Paragon Connection

Dire animals in Oreka are not a separate species. They are the direct descendants of Paragons — the immortal spirit-guardians that embody the ideal of each animal species. When a Paragon mates (a rare event, occurring perhaps once in a century), the offspring is a dire animal: a creature that carries a fragment of the Paragon's elemental resonance in its blood, expressed as greater size, enhanced physical capabilities, and a lifespan far exceeding its mundane counterpart.

A dire animal is always born to a mundane mother — the Paragon's essence overwhelms the natural template, producing a creature that is recognisably the same species but pushed to extremes. A dire wolf born to a normal she-wolf is unmistakable from birth: larger, more aggressive, and radiating a Kin-sense signature that is stronger and more defined than any normal animal's Harmony signal.

Dire animals are rare. Each Paragon produces at most a handful of offspring per century, and not all survive to adulthood. Their rarity is what makes them significant — a dire animal in a region signals the presence (or recent presence) of a Paragon, which in turn signals that the elemental balance in that area is stable enough to support one.

**Kin-Sense Signature:** All dire animals register on Kin-sense as enhanced versions of their mundane counterparts' Harmony signal — the same warmth, the same species-signature, but louder, sharper, more defined. Kin who detect a dire animal's resonance instinctively recognise it as something more than a normal beast. A DC 15 Kin-sense check identifies the "Paragon-echo" — the trace of the sire's elemental identity running through the offspring's signal.

**The Harmony:** Dire animals possess an amplified version of the Harmony. Where a normal wolf senses pack-mates within a few hundred feet, a dire wolf senses all wolves within 2 miles. This extended awareness makes dire animals natural pack-leaders and herd-dominants — other animals of the same species instinctively defer to them.

**Elemental Affinity:** Each dire animal carries the elemental affinity of its Paragon sire, expressed through its physiology and habitat preference. The specific affinity depends on the species and the regional Paragon that sired it.

## General Dire Animal Notes

**Encountering Dire Animals:** A DM placing a dire animal in an encounter should consider the Paragon connection. The presence of a dire animal suggests a Paragon is active in the region, which means the local ecosystem is healthy and elementally balanced. Killing a dire animal has no mechanical effect on the Paragon, but it removes one of the Paragon's rare offspring from the world — an act that Hasura druids, Mytroan herders, and Warg communities all consider spiritually significant.

**Dire Animals and the Dómnathar:** Dómnathar tunnelling operations occasionally kill Paragons or drive them from their territories. Regions where dire animal populations have declined are, for naturalists and druids, an early warning sign of Dómnathar subterranean expansion — the Paragons leave before the tunnels reach the surface.

**Dire Animals as Mounts:** No culture in Oreka rides dire animals. Their Paragon heritage makes them too independent, too intelligent (by animal standards), and too culturally significant to serve as beasts of burden. A Mytroan who attempted to break a dire horse (if one existed) would face social consequences equivalent to desecrating a shrine.
