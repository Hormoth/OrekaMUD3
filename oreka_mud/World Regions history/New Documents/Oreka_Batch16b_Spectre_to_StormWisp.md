# SPECTRE

The Ashen Hollows are the places where fire died — the volcanic valleys of the Scorchspire foothills where eruptions once poured lava and now pour nothing, where the ground is cold and grey and the trees are petrified stumps. Nothing lives here. That is the first sign that something dead does. The shape that rises from the grey earth is not smoke — it is darker than smoke, more defined, and it moves with the deliberate purpose of something that remembers being alive and hates you for still being so. Its face is a rictus of fury frozen in shadow, and where it passes, the temperature drops, not to cold but to *absence* — the heat of life draining away into something that has none.

### Combat

A spectre is an energy-drain predator — faster, more intelligent, and more aggressive than a shadow, with the ability to drain actual levels rather than merely ability scores. Its incorporeal touch deals 1d8 damage plus two negative levels, and any humanoid killed by a spectre rises as a new spectre under the killer's control.

Spectres are tactically sophisticated (Int 14). They attack at night (sunlight renders them powerless), targeting spellcasters first (the most dangerous opponents and the most devastating level-drain targets), and use their fly speed (80 ft., perfect) to maintain air superiority against melee-only parties.

The unnatural aura (30-foot radius) causes animals to panic, depriving the party of mounts and animal companions before combat begins.

### Special Abilities

**Energy Drain (Su):** A spectre's incorporeal touch inflicts two negative levels. The DC to remove the negative levels is 15. The save DC is Charisma-based.

**Create Spawn (Su):** Any humanoid killed by a spectre's energy drain rises as a spectre 1d4 rounds later under the control of the spectre that created it. The new spectre retains none of its former abilities.

**Sunlight Powerlessness (Ex):** A spectre caught in sunlight cannot attack and flees. It is not destroyed but is effectively helpless.

**Unnatural Aura (Su):** Animals will not voluntarily approach within 30 feet. Animals within the aura panic (as *fear*) and remain panicked for the duration of the spectre's presence.

**Extinguished Fire Resonance (Su):** A spectre registers on Kin-sense as fire that has been extinguished — the Lady of Fire's resonance inverted, heat replaced by the memory of heat. Detectable at 60 feet. Rarozhki fire-smiths describe it as "the forge going cold while you're still standing in it" — the visceral wrongness of a fire-signature that radiates *loss* instead of warmth. The resonance is unique to the Ashen Hollows — spectres formed elsewhere do not carry it; only those born from the grief of extinguished volcanic energy.

### Society

Spectres in Oreka are concentrated in the Ashen Hollows — the dead volcanic valleys where the Lady of Fire's presence once burned and no longer does. The Hollows are the Scorchspires' graveyard: places where volcanic activity ceased centuries or millennia ago, leaving landscapes of petrified forests, cold lava-flows, and grey ash-fields. The elemental grief of these locations — fire-resonance that remembers being alive — produces spectres of unusual power and malice.

Rarozhki Dwarves who operate near the Ashen Hollows treat them as exclusion zones. Rarozhki volcanic-cartography maps Hollow boundaries with the same precision applied to active volcanic zones — a living volcano is dangerous; a dead volcano is haunted. Rarozhki border-patrols carry sunlight-producing magical items (typically *daylight* wands or alchemical sunrods) as standard equipment when operating near Hollows.

Taraf-Imro warriors who range through the Scorchspire foothills encounter spectres and fight them with a combination of positive-energy channelling (Taraf-Imro warrior-traditions include basic turn undead training for senior fighters) and tactical discipline — keeping formation to prevent spectres from isolating individuals.

### Lore

**DC 17 (Knowledge [religion]):** Spectres are powerful incorporeal undead that drain levels through touch. Two negative levels per hit. Humanoids killed by spectres rise as new spectres. Sunlight renders them powerless but does not destroy them.

**DC 22:** The unnatural aura panics animals within 30 feet. Spectres are tactically intelligent and target spellcasters first. Rarozhki map Ashen Hollow boundaries as exclusion zones.

### Combat

A spider eater is a specialist predator — its entire biology is built around hunting giant spiders and implanting larvae in them. Against non-spider targets, it attacks only in self-defence or if extremely hungry, using its sting (which delivers a paralytic poison) and bite.

Against spiders, a spider eater is devastatingly efficient: dive from above (Flyby Attack if feat is taken at advancement), sting to deliver the implant, and withdraw before the spider can react. Freedom of movement makes it immune to web entanglement — the spider's primary defence is useless.

### Special Abilities

**Implant (Ex):** A spider eater can implant an egg in a paralysed or helpless creature (preferably a spider). The egg hatches in 1d4 weeks, and the larva devours the host from within (dealing 2d6 damage per day until the host dies). *Remove disease* kills the egg.

**Poison (Ex):** Sting; Fortitude DC 16; initial damage paralysis for 1d8+5 minutes; no secondary damage. The save DC is Constitution-based.

**Canopy-Predator Resonance (Su):** Spider eaters register on Kin-sense as upper-canopy wind — the Youngest Brother's domain filtered through forest architecture. Detectable at 30 feet. Hasura elves in the canopy recognise spider eater resonance and consider the creatures beneficial (they control giant spider populations that would otherwise threaten Hasura canopy-settlements).

### Society

Spider eaters are the Great Forests' aerial specialist-predators — large, fast-flying creatures that control giant spider populations through parasitic reproduction. Their ecological role is critical: without spider eaters, giant spider populations would expand unchecked, overwhelming the mid-canopy and threatening both Hasura canopy-settlements and Pasua ground-routes.

Hasura elves consider spider eaters allies and never hunt them. Hasura canopy-settlements near spider eater nesting-sites benefit from dramatically reduced spider-web density in the surrounding canopy. Pasua scouts value spider eaters for the same reason — a forest section with active spider eater populations is safer to traverse than one without.

### Lore

**DC 15 (Knowledge [arcana]):** Spider eaters are Large, flying predators that hunt giant spiders. Their sting delivers paralytic poison. They implant eggs in paralysed prey — the larvae consume the host from within. *Remove disease* kills the egg.

**DC 20:** Spider eaters are immune to web entanglement (freedom of movement). Hasura elves consider them beneficial allies that control spider populations. They nest in the upper canopy.

**DC 25:** Spider eater resonance is upper-canopy wind. Their ecological role is critical to forest health. A forest section with active spider eater populations has dramatically reduced giant spider density.

Something has corrupted this stretch of river, and the corruption has a voice. It speaks from the water: a woman's voice, cultured, persuasive, and utterly, chillingly reasonable, explaining why you should come closer, why you should listen, why resistance to the inevitable is wasteful. The voice belongs to a creature coiled in the corrupted water — fifteen feet of scaled serpent-body supporting a human-like face with eyes that glow faint green and a smile that promises understanding it has no intention of delivering.

### Combat

A spirit naga is a sorcerer-predator that fights with spells, charm, and poison in combination. It opens with charming gaze to neutralise the strongest-willed opponent, then casts offensive spells (*lightning bolt*, *fireball*) to damage the party while its charmed puppet fights on the naga's behalf. Its bite delivers Constitution-damaging poison, used against targets that resist charm.

### Special Abilities

**Charming Gaze (Su):** 30 feet, DC 20 Will save or be charmed as *charm person* (caster level 9th). The save DC is Charisma-based (+2 Ability Focus).

**Poison (Ex):** Bite; Fortitude DC 18; initial and secondary damage 1d6 Con. The save DC is Constitution-based.

**Spells:** A spirit naga casts spells as a 7th-level sorcerer (and can also cast spells from the cleric list as arcane spells):
- 0 — *daze, detect magic, mage hand, message, ray of frost, read magic, resistance*
- 1st (7/day) — *charm person* (DC 15), *cure light wounds, divine favour, magic missile, shield of faith*
- 2nd (7/day) — *cat's grace, invisibility, hold person* (DC 16)
- 3rd (5/day) — *displacement, lightning bolt* (DC 17)

**Corrupted Current Resonance (Su):** A spirit naga registers on Kin-sense as water *claimed* — river-current no longer flowing naturally but directed by an intelligence. Detectable at 60 feet. The resonance extends through the corrupted waterway itself — Kin-sense users wading in naga-controlled water feel the corruption as a subtle wrongness, "water that knows you're there and is deciding what to do about it." Pekakarlik river-navigators describe the sensation as "the river getting a personality."

### Society

Spirit nagas are territorial water-predators that claim stretches of waterway and corrupt them through sustained magical presence. They are intelligent, manipulative, and patient — a spirit naga establishes itself in a remote tributary, extends its corrupted influence over months or years, and eventually dominates a stretch of waterway long enough to threaten the communities downstream.

Pekakarlik river-navigators are the Kin culture most directly affected by spirit nagas. A spirit naga on a Great River tributary corrupts the water-quality, drives away river serpents (which will not tolerate corrupted water), and threatens Pekakarlik trade-routes that depend on clean, navigable waterways. Pekakarlik anti-naga response is coordinated and ruthless: when a spirit naga is identified, the Pekakarlik assemble a river-clearing team — experienced fighters, a Kin-sense specialist to track the corrupted resonance to the naga's lair, and a cleric or spellcaster capable of countering the charming gaze.

Eruskan river-cities downstream of naga-corrupted tributaries experience water-quality degradation that affects public health. Eruskan civic authorities maintain bounties on spirit nagas — significant rewards for confirmed naga kills.

River serpents and spirit nagas are natural enemies. A river serpent will attack a spirit naga on sight — the corruption of the Lady of the Sea's domain is an existential affront. A waterway stretch occupied by a spirit naga is, by definition, a stretch without river serpent protection.

### Lore

**DC 19 (Knowledge [arcana]):** Spirit nagas are Large aberrations that corrupt waterways. They charm with their gaze, cast spells as 7th-level sorcerers, and deliver Constitution-damaging poison. They are intelligent and manipulative.

**DC 24:** Spirit nagas corrupt water through sustained magical presence, driving away river serpents and threatening downstream communities. Pekakarlik assemble specialised river-clearing teams. Charming gaze DC 20 requires strong-willed front-line fighters.

**DC 29:** Corrupted current resonance extends through the claimed waterway — Kin-sense detects the wrongness in the water itself. River serpents and spirit nagas are natural enemies. Eruskan cities maintain bounties on spirit nagas.

### Combat

A stirge has one tactic: attach and drain. It makes a touch attack to latch onto exposed skin, then drains 1d4 Constitution per round. A single stirge is an annoyance; a storm of 9–14 is a genuine threat — multiple simultaneous Constitution drains can incapacitate or kill even experienced combatants.

### Special Abilities

**Attach (Ex):** A stirge that hits with a touch attack latches onto the target. An attached stirge is effectively grappling but has a +12 racial bonus on grapple checks (already included). An attached stirge can be removed with a successful grapple check or by dealing damage to it (AC 12 while attached).

**Blood Drain (Ex):** An attached stirge drains blood, dealing 1d4 Constitution damage per round. A stirge that drains 4 points of Constitution detaches and flies away, sated.

### Society

Stirges are the Deepwater Marches' most ubiquitous blood-parasites — tiny, numerous, and relentless. They are not dangerous individually, but their numbers make them a persistent environmental hazard that every culture operating in the Deepwater Marches has developed countermeasures for.

Halfling marsh-communities treat stirge management as routine pest-control — stirge-breeding pools are identified and drained or treated with herbal insecticides, and sleeping-shelters are equipped with fine-mesh netting. Halfling children learn stirge-avoidance before they learn to swim.

Pasua scouts crossing the Deepwater Marches carry stirge-repellent (a pungent herbal compound rubbed on exposed skin) and wear high-collared, tight-cuffed clothing. The Pasua term for stirge translates as "flying leech."

### Lore

### A Note on Stone Giants in Oreka

Stone giants in Oreka are **not Brutes**. They are the last surviving natural descendants of the original Giants — the Firstborn who shaped the world during the Age of Giants and vanished during the Thalorien (the Fading). Stone giants carry a diluted but genuine version of the Giants' elemental heritage, and their presence in the Giant's Teeth is the strongest remaining evidence that the original Giants were real beings of flesh and stone rather than purely mythological figures.

The distinction from Brutes is absolute and critical: Brutes (Hrokk, Goreham, Charjaw, Fogwrack) are Dómnathar-manufactured war-creatures that *look* like giants but are artificial, feral, and elementally void. Stone giants are natural beings with genuine elemental resonance, cultural traditions, and an ancestral connection to the world's geological history.

### Combat

Stone giants fight from elevated positions, using rock throwing (180-foot range, 2d8+8 damage) with tactical precision — they calculate trajectories that account for cover, movement, and the tendency of targets to dodge in predictable directions. A stone giant on a cliff-face above an enemy is among the most difficult tactical problems in Oreka: attacking uphill against a creature that can throw boulders with the accuracy of a siege engine.

In melee, stone giants are straightforward — greatclub attacks with Str 27 deal devastating damage, and rock catching (they can catch thrown boulders, siege stones, and similar projectiles) makes ranged counter-attack difficult.

### Special Abilities

**Rock Throwing (Ex):** Range 180 feet. 2d8+8 damage.

**Rock Catching (Ex):** A stone giant can catch Small, Medium, or Large rocks (or similar projectiles) as a free action once per round with a DC 20 Reflex save.

**Giant Remnant Resonance (Su):** A stone giant registers on Kin-sense as the original Giants' earth-resonance — diminished and fragmented, but recognisably derived from the Firstborn's connection to the Lord of Stone. Detectable at 120 feet. The sensation is profoundly affecting for earth-attuned Kin: Vestri Dwarves describe encountering stone giant resonance as "hearing your grandfather's voice in the mountain" — familiar, ancestral, and laden with the grief of something great that has been diminished. Stone giant resonance is the strongest evidence available through Kin-sense that the original Giants were real.

### Society

Stone giants live in small tribal communities in the high Giant's Teeth — cliff-dwellings carved into mountain-faces, cave-complexes reinforced with dry-stone walls, and open-air camps on sheltered plateaus. They are the last natural Giant-descended people in Oreka, and their way of life reflects a culture in long, slow decline — a people who remember (through oral tradition) that they were once part of something vast and have accepted, with the patient resignation of stone, that the vastness is gone.

Vestri Dwarves maintain the most complex relationship with stone giants. The Vestri mine the same mountains that stone giants inhabit, and territorial disputes over specific valleys and cliff-faces are ongoing. However, the relationship is not purely adversarial — Vestri and stone giants have coexisted for millennia, and some Vestri mining-communities maintain trade-relationships with neighbouring stone giant tribes (Vestri metalwork for stone giant raw ore and gemstones).

The Vestri relationship with stone giants is deeply complicated by the Vestri awareness of what stone giants *represent*. The Vestri venerate the original Giants as the world's builders, and stone giants — diminished, feral, but undeniably descended from the Firstborn — are living relics of that venerated past. Killing a stone giant weighs heavily on Vestri conscience in a way that killing a Brute (which is a Dómnathar manufacturing abomination) does not.

Orean mountain-communities occasionally encounter stone giant hunting-parties in the foothills and maintain cautious distance. Orean tradition includes stone giants in the category of "the old ones" — beings from before Kin-memory that belong to the mountains in ways the Kin do not.

### Lore

**DC 18 (Knowledge [nature]):** Stone giants are Large, intelligent natural giants of the Giant's Teeth. They throw boulders with devastating accuracy (180-foot range) and can catch thrown projectiles. They are NOT Brutes — they are genuine descendants of the original Giants.

**DC 23:** Stone giants carry diluted Giant-remnant resonance — the strongest remaining evidence of the original Giants' elemental heritage. Vestri mining-communities maintain trade-relationships with some tribes. The Vestri relationship is complicated by veneration of the original Giants.

**DC 28:** Stone giant resonance is the Firstborn's earth-attunement, diminished but recognisable. Earth-attuned Kin find the sensation profoundly moving. Stone giant oral traditions include memories of the Thalorien. Their culture represents the last living connection to the Age of Giants.

### Combat

Storm wisps do not fight tactically — they react to stimuli. They are drawn to campfires, metal equipment, and living creatures, and deliver painful but usually non-lethal electric shocks through contact. A swirl of 3–6 wisps is more dangerous, as their static bursts can chain.

A storm wisp does not intentionally attack Kin — the shocks are more mischief than malice. However, the damage is real, and metal armour or weapons conduct the electricity with enhanced effect (–2 on saves against static burst for creatures wearing metal).

### Special Abilities

**Electric Touch (Su):** 1d6 electricity damage. On a critical hit (19–20), the target must succeed on a DC 13 Fortitude save or be stunned for 1 round. The save DC is Charisma-based.

**Static Burst (Su):** Once per day, a storm wisp can release a 10-foot-radius burst of electrical energy dealing 2d6 electricity damage (DC 13 Reflex save for half). Metal armour or weapons impose –2 on the save. The save DC is Constitution-based.

**Natural Invisibility (Su):** Storm wisps are naturally invisible during calm weather. During storms, rain, or high winds, they become visible as crackling blue-white spheres. *See invisibility* or *true seeing* reveals them at all times.

**Wind Dependent (Ex):** In completely calm, windless conditions, storm wisps take 1 point of damage per round and cannot use static burst.

**Gaseous Form (Su):** At will, a storm wisp can become ionised air (as *gaseous form*) retaining fly speed. It cannot attack in this form.

**Raw Static Resonance (Su):** Storm wisps register on Kin-sense as raw air-element crackle — faint, intermittent, like the feeling before a lightning strike. Detectable at 15 feet per wisp; a swirl of six generates a combined field at 90 feet. Pasua elves and Mytroan wind-callers find the sensation delightful; earth-attuned Kin (Vestri, Pekakarlik) find it itchy and distracting.

### Society

Storm wisps are the common tier of the Storm Wisp Elemental Sovereign family — tiny, mischievous air-elementals that represent the Youngest Brother's domain at its most playful and least controlled. They are the most commonly encountered Elemental Sovereign creatures and the most deeply integrated into everyday Kin life during storm-season.

Pasua elves have the warmest relationship with storm wisps. Pasua charm-singers have songs that soothe agitated wisps, and Pasua scouts consider a swirl of wisps in the canopy a sign of healthy forest-air. The Pasua term for storm wisps translates as "the Brother's laughter" — affectionate, slightly exasperated.

Mytroan wind-callers on the Eternal Steppe treat storm wisps as weather-omens. Storm wisp activity precedes thunderstorms by hours, and Mytroan herders who observe wisp-behaviour can predict storm intensity and direction with remarkable accuracy. Some Mytroan clans claim specific wisps as guardian-spirits, though whether a wisp with Int 4 can meaningfully serve as a guardian is debatable.

Storm wisps are the youngest form of a family that includes Storm Heralds (Large, CR 8 — vaguely avian shapes of compressed wind and lightning) and Gale Sovereigns (Gargantuan, CR 15 — living, thinking storms that remember the Age of Giants). This lifecycle means every storm wisp is a potential Gale Sovereign, though the transition takes millennia.
