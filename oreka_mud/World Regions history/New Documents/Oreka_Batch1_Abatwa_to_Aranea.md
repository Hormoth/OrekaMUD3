# ABATWA

Crouched in the shadow of a single blade of steppe-grass, nearly invisible against the red earth, a figure no taller than a man's thumb draws a bow strung with spider-silk. Its arrow — a splinter of bone dipped in something that smells faintly of turned soil — is aimed with the steady confidence of a hunter who has never missed.

### Combat

Abatwa are ambush hunters who fight only when cornered or enraged. A hunting band conceals itself in tall grass, waits until a target is surrounded, then fires a volley of poisoned arrows from positions the target cannot see. They do not press attacks against creatures that flee — their poison does the work over time. If an Abatwa is spotted and insulted (see Size Rage), it attacks with suicidal fury, ignoring tactical advantage entirely.

Against large game, Abatwa hunting bands use coordinated volleys: all members fire simultaneously at the same target, relying on poison accumulation rather than raw damage. A band of six Abatwa can bring down a bison in under an hour this way without ever being seen.

### Special Abilities

**Steppe-Thorn Poison (Ex):** Abatwa coat their arrows in a toxin derived from a steppe plant whose roots tap into the deep earth. Injury; Fortitude DC 12; initial damage 1 Str; secondary damage 1d2 Str. The save DC is Constitution-based and includes a +2 racial bonus reflecting the Abatwa's long refinement of this poison. The poison is ineffective against creatures of size Huge or larger unless at least four doses are delivered within 1 minute, in which case the DC increases by +1 per additional dose beyond the first.

**Size Rage (Ex):** An Abatwa that is mocked for its size, stepped on (even accidentally), or told by a creature that it was only noticed "just now" flies into an uncontrollable fury. The Abatwa gains +2 Strength, +2 Constitution, and –2 AC for 1d4+1 rounds. During this rage, the Abatwa attacks the offending creature exclusively and does not retreat, fight defensively, or use total defense. It will pursue the target for up to 1 mile. This rage cannot be ended voluntarily and is not a magical effect — it is pure indignation.

**Elemental Affinity (Su):** The Abatwa are creatures of deep earth and old stone. Their elemental affinity is to the Lord of Stone. This manifests as a faint tremor in the ground when a tribe gathers — too subtle for most creatures to notice, but detectable with a DC 20 Kin-sense check or tremorsense. Abatwa gain a +2 racial bonus on saves against spells with the Earth descriptor (this bonus is already factored into their saves in relevant situations) and their poison interacts with earth and stone in unusual ways: creatures standing on bare earth when the poison takes effect suffer a –1 penalty on the Fortitude save.

**Fey Resonance (Su):** To Kin-sense, an individual Abatwa registers as a faint, dry warmth — like sun-baked clay or the smell of dust after rain. The signal is so small that detecting a single Abatwa requires a DC 25 Kin-sense check. A hunting band of six or more produces a stronger signal (DC 15), which Kin describe as "the ground humming to itself." Abatwa are Oreka-native fey and are part of the world's elemental fabric; their resonance is natural and does not produce the void-signature associated with extraplanar creatures.

**Ant Rider (Ex):** Abatwa can ride Tiny or smaller vermin (typically giant ants or common steppe ants enhanced by the Abatwa's fey presence) as mounts. While mounted, an Abatwa gains +10 ft. to its base speed and can use the ant's burrow speed (if any) to enter and exit anthills. An ant ridden by an Abatwa is treated as a war-trained mount and does not flee from combat. Abatwa tribes maintain colonies of semi-domesticated ants that serve as mounts, pack animals, and perimeter guards.

**Unseen Passage (Ex):** An Abatwa's extraordinary size and natural camouflage make it nearly undetectable in its home terrain. An Abatwa that is stationary in tall grass or steppe terrain can only be detected visually by creatures with a Spot check result of 30 or higher, or by children under the age of four, who see Abatwa automatically (the fey have no explanation for this and find it intensely annoying). This is an extraordinary ability and is not affected by see invisibility or true seeing, though effects that detect life force (such as deathwatch or Kin-sense) function normally.

### Society

Abatwa are nomadic hunters who follow game across the Eternal Steppe in tribes of twenty to sixty individuals. They have no permanent settlement — when a kill is made, the tribe camps at the carcass until every scrap is consumed, then moves on. Despite their size, they are capable of bringing down prey many hundreds of times their mass through patience and poison.

Their dwellings are temporary bivouacs inside anthills, whose interior corridors they decorate with tiny mosaics made from coloured seeds and mineral dust — the only art form the Abatwa practice, and the only evidence they leave behind. Steppe-Kin herders occasionally find these mosaics in abandoned anthills and consider them powerful good-luck charms.

Among the Warg clans of the eastern Steppe, the Abatwa are known as "the ground's own hunters" and treated with careful respect. Warg outriders leave small offerings of meat at anthills before long journeys — not out of worship, but out of the practical understanding that a Warg who accidentally steps on an Abatwa camp will be hunted by a furious, invisible war-party for the next three days. Steppe-Kin mothers teach children to answer any tiny voice asking "where did you see me?" with "from far away, on the mountain" — the correct response that averts an Abatwa's size rage.

Abatwa have no relationship with the Dómnathar and no awareness of the threat beneath the world. They are fey of the surface — old, small, and deeply rooted in the Steppe's earth. Their concerns are game, territory, and pride.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (nature) can learn the following about Abatwa.

**DC 12:** Abatwa are tiny fey of the Eternal Steppe who hunt with poisoned arrows. They are nearly impossible to see in tall grass.

**DC 17:** Abatwa are fiercely proud and violently sensitive about their size. If encountered, one should claim to have seen them from far away. They ride ants and live inside anthills.

**DC 22:** Their poison is derived from a steppe-thorn plant with deep roots and is more effective against creatures standing on bare earth. A tribe of Abatwa produces a faint Kin-sense resonance like warm stone; a solitary Abatwa is nearly undetectable even to Kin-sense.

**DC 27:** Abatwa mosaics found in abandoned anthills are considered good-luck tokens by Warg herders. The Abatwa's fey nature is tied to the Lord of Stone — they are earth-fey, not plant-fey, and their connection to the Steppe runs deeper than the grassroots.

# ABOLETH

The water darkens without cause. Far below, where the light of the surface is a rumour, something vast turns in the silt — a shape like a beached whale wrapped in mucus, its three vertical eyes glowing with a cold intelligence that did not originate in this world. Tentacles trail behind it like the trains of a drowned king's robe. It came here with the Deceivers. It chose to stay. And it has been building ever since.

### Combat

An aboleth attacks by flailing with its long, slimy tentacles, each coated in a mucus that transforms living flesh into a clear, spongy membrane. It prefers to enslave opponents mentally rather than destroy them physically — a dead servant is useless, but an enslaved one can breathe water, serve for years, and be discarded when spent.

Aboleths open combat with Enslave against the most dangerous-looking opponent. They can identify spellcasters by observing behaviour and have generations of experience reading humanoid body language — both from their homeplane and from centuries of studying Kin from the deep water. If enslavement fails, they target that creature with concentrated tentacle attacks to convert its skin and force it underwater, where it becomes dependent on the aboleth's mucus cloud to breathe. Aboleths retreat into deep water when seriously threatened. They are patient beyond any mortal measure and will simply wait decades for a better opportunity.

An enclave of aboleths coordinates through telepathy, designating targets so that no two aboleths attempt to enslave the same creature. Their psionic illusions are layered — one aboleth creates the lure while the others position for ambush.

### Special Abilities

**Enslave (Su):** Three times per day, an aboleth can attempt to enslave any one living creature within 30 feet. The target must succeed on a Will save (DC 17) or be affected as though by a dominate person spell (caster level 16th). An enslaved creature obeys the aboleth's telepathic commands until freed by remove curse (DC 17), and can attempt a new Will save every 24 hours to break free. The save DC is Charisma-based. The aboleth can command enslaved creatures telepathically at any distance on the same body of water.

This ability made the aboleths invaluable to the Dómnathar during the Deceivers' War. An aboleth positioned in coastal waters could enslave harbourmasters, merchant captains, and coastal leaders without the Houses risking a single operative above ground. In exchange, the Houses kept the aboleths fed with a steady supply of slaves and thralls, delivered to the deep water on schedule. It was the most efficient intelligence network the Dómnathar ever operated — and the one they lost when the aboleths decided they no longer needed partners.

**Psionics (Sp):** At will — hypnotic pattern (DC 15), illusory wall (DC 17), mirage arcana (DC 18), persistent image (DC 18), programmed image (DC 19), project image (DC 20), veil (DC 19). Effective caster level 16th. The save DCs are Charisma-based. Aboleths use these abilities to construct elaborate false environments in the deep water — phantom coral cities, illusory treasure hoards, simulated sunlit shallows — to lure creatures into their territory. Na'wasua divers call these "the bright traps" and teach their children that any light in the deep trenches is a lie.

**Slime (Ex):** A blow from an aboleth's tentacle can cause a terrible transformation. A creature hit must succeed on a Fortitude save (DC 19) or begin to transform over 1d4+1 minutes, its skin becoming a clear, slimy membrane. A transformed creature must remain submerged in cool, fresh water or take 1d12 points of damage every 10 minutes. The slime can be removed by remove disease before the transformation is complete; afterward, it requires heal or mass heal. The save DC is Constitution-based.

**Mucus Cloud (Ex):** An aboleth underwater surrounds itself with a viscous cloud of mucus roughly 1 foot thick. Any creature coming into contact with and inhaling this substance must succeed on a Fortitude save (DC 19) or lose the ability to breathe air for the next 3 hours. Affected creatures can breathe water freely during this time. Renewed contact and a failed save extends the effect for another 3 hours. The save DC is Constitution-based.

**Abyssal Silence (Su):** Like all creatures that came through the Assault Gate, aboleths do not register on Kin-sense. They produce the same void-signature as the Dómnathar — an absolute absence where a living presence should be. However, an aboleth's void has a quality that experienced Silence Readers describe as "deeper" than a standard Dómnathar void — less like an empty room and more like a hole in the ocean floor with no bottom. A DC 25 Kin-sense check allows a Kin creature to distinguish an aboleth's void from a Dómnathar's, though this distinction has practical value only to those who know what both feel like.

**Alien Memory (Ex):** Aboleths possess perfect, inherited memory stretching back through every ancestor to the first of their kind — not on Oreka, but on whatever plane the Dómnathar host originated from. An aboleth remembers its ancestral home, the transit through the Assault Gate, the early campaigns of the Deceivers' War, and every year since in unbroken detail.

Any creature that successfully communicates with an aboleth (through Enslave, telepathy, or magical means) can attempt to extract historical information. The aboleth answers truthfully when it chooses to answer at all — it has no reason to lie about facts, but it has excellent reasons to withhold them. Information about Dómnathar House politics, Assault Gate mechanics, or the aboleths' own enclave locations requires a DC 25 Diplomacy or Intimidate check, and even then the aboleth reveals only what serves its interests. Information about surface-world history is given freely, because the aboleth considers Kin affairs beneath strategic concern.

### Society

Aboleths came through the Assault Gate as part of the original Dómnathar host — not as soldiers, but as assets. Their psionic abilities made them the Deceivers' most effective tool for infiltrating surface populations: an aboleth placed in coastal waters could enslave harbourmasters, merchant captains, and coastal leaders without the Houses risking a single operative above ground. In exchange, the Houses kept the aboleths fed with a steady supply of slaves and thralls, delivered to the deep water on schedule.

The arrangement held for as long as the Deceivers' War went well. When the war stalled and the Dómnathar host fractured into the Eleven Great Houses, the aboleths made a calculation. The Houses were weakened, divided, and increasingly focused on fighting each other. The supply of thralls became unreliable. And the aboleths had spent centuries studying Oreka's oceans, mapping its deep trenches, and quietly enslaving enough aquatic creatures to sustain themselves independently.

They left. Not dramatically — aboleths do nothing dramatically — but with the slow, inevitable certainty of water finding its own level. They withdrew from Dómnathar territory, broke telepathic contact with their former handlers, and sank into the deepest waters of the Apelian Sea. There, they built.

An aboleth enclave is a drowned fortress carved from trench rock by enslaved labour, hidden behind layers of psionic illusion and guarded by thralls who do not know they are guarding anything. Each enclave operates as an independent power — a twelfth faction in a world that knows of only eleven, answerable to no House, no patron god, and no authority but the eldest aboleth's inherited memory. They are extremely rare; there may be fewer than a dozen aboleths in all of Oreka's waters. But a single aboleth with a century's worth of enslaved agents is a strategic threat that rivals a Great House's surface operations.

The Dómnathar want them back — or dead. The aboleths know too much about House operations, Assault Gate transit, and the early war campaigns. Several Houses have made attempts to reestablish contact or eliminate rogue aboleths. All such attempts have failed, because the Houses must send operatives into deep water to reach the enclaves, and in deep water, the aboleth is god.

Na'wasua oral traditions preserve warnings about "the old things in the black water" — but these warnings predate any Na'wasua understanding of the Dómnathar. The Na'wasua do not know that the creatures in their deepest waters came from the same place as the Deceivers. If they did, the political implications would reshape every coastal Kin nation's understanding of the threat they face.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (the planes) can learn the following about aboleths.

**DC 12:** Aboleths are massive aquatic creatures found in the deepest waters. They have tentacles that secrete a dangerous slime and are highly intelligent.

**DC 17:** An aboleth can mentally enslave other creatures and force them to serve as thralls indefinitely. Its slime transforms a victim's skin so they can only breathe underwater, creating permanent dependence. They produce a void on Kin-sense, like the Dómnathar.

**DC 22:** Aboleths are extraplanar — they came through the Assault Gate with the original Dómnathar host and served as psionic intelligence assets during the Deceivers' War. They possess perfect inherited memory of every ancestor, including memories of the transit itself and the campaigns that followed.

**DC 27:** The aboleths broke with the Houses after the Dómnathar host fractured and now operate independent enclaves in the deepest Apelian trenches. These enclaves rival the Great Houses in intelligence-gathering capability. The Dómnathar consider rogue aboleths a significant security threat, as they possess detailed knowledge of House operations, Assault Gate mechanics, and early war strategies that no other living creature retains.

# ADZE

The lamp at the window gutters. A firefly drifts in through the gap beneath the door — just one, glowing faintly amber in the dark. It lands on the sleeping child's arm. By morning, the child is pale, feverish, and will not wake. The firefly is gone. There is no wound to find.

### Combat

An adze does not fight if it can help it. It operates entirely through stealth — entering dwellings in firefly form, draining blood from sleeping victims, and departing before dawn. If discovered in firefly form and physically captured (grappled or trapped in a container), the adze is forced to revert to its true form: a hunched, gaunt humanoid with ashen skin, long fingers ending in dark nails, and a lipless mouth crowded with needle-teeth. In this form it is vulnerable and knows it. A cornered adze fights with desperate ferocity, using its claws and bite to create an opening to flee, then shifting back to firefly form at the first opportunity.

An adze never engages a group in open combat. If confronted by more than one armed creature, it flees immediately. They are predators of the sleeping and the alone, not warriors.

### Special Abilities

**Blood Drain (Ex):** In firefly form, an adze that lands on a sleeping or helpless creature can drain blood as a full-round action. The victim takes 1d2 points of Constitution damage and does not wake unless they succeed on a DC 14 Fortitude save. On a failed save, the victim remains asleep throughout the feeding and wakes feeling weak and feverish with no memory of the attack. The save DC is Charisma-based. An adze can feed multiple times in a single night, but typically drains no more than 2 points of Constitution per victim before moving on — enough to sustain itself without killing, which would draw attention.

An adze that has not fed for more than 48 hours becomes desperate and careless, taking 4 or more Constitution from a single victim if given the opportunity. Such feedings can kill children and Small creatures outright.

**Possession (Su):** Once per day, an adze in its true form can attempt to possess a humanoid creature within 5 feet. The target must succeed on a Will save (DC 13) or become possessed, as the spell magic jar but without a receptacle — the adze's essence enters the host and the adze's body disappears. While possessing a host, the adze has access to the host's physical abilities and skills but uses its own mental ability scores, alignment, and special qualities. The host is aware of the possession but cannot act independently.

A possessed host can be identified by a DC 20 Sense Motive check (they exhibit subtle changes in temperament — increased jealousy, irrational resentment toward family members, loss of appetite replaced by cravings for raw meat or blood). The possession can be ended by protection from evil, remove curse (DC 14), or by reducing the host to 0 hit points (the adze is ejected and materialises in its true form, staggered for 1 round). The save DC is Charisma-based.

**Disease (Su):** A creature that has been drained by an adze and fails a DC 13 Fortitude save contracts marsh fever — incubation 1d3 days; damage 1d3 Str and 1d3 Con. This disease is supernatural in origin and cannot be treated by mundane means, though remove disease eliminates it normally. The save DC is Charisma-based.

**Firefly Form (Su):** As a standard action, an adze can assume the form of a Diminutive firefly (or similar marsh insect — beetle, moth, mosquito). In this form, the adze gains fly 40 ft. (perfect), a +8 bonus on Hide checks (total +21), and can squeeze through any opening that is not airtight — keyholes, the gap beneath a door, cracks in shutters, gaps in thatching. The adze cannot attack with claws or bite in firefly form, but can use Blood Drain on sleeping or helpless creatures. It retains its special qualities and mental ability scores. The adze reverts to its true form if reduced to 0 hit points, grappled, or physically contained (trapped in a jar, pinned under a bowl, etc.). The adze can remain in firefly form indefinitely and spends most of its existence in this shape.

**Abyssal Silence (Su):** The adze does not register on Kin-sense. In firefly form, this is functionally undetectable — no Kin expects to sense an insect, so the void passes unnoticed. In true form, however, the absence is stark: a humanoid-shaped creature that reads as empty space. A Kin creature within 30 feet of an adze in true form can detect the void with a DC 20 Kin-sense check, or DC 15 if they possess the Silence Reader feat. The void-signature is identical to other Dómnathar-summoned creatures, because that is exactly what the adze is.

**Unbarred Passage (Su):** In firefly form, an adze can pass through any non-magical physical barrier that is not airtight. Doors, shutters, thatched roofs, and stone walls with mortar gaps all fail to keep it out. Only an airtight seal, a wall with no cracks, or a magical barrier (such as protection from evil, magic circle against evil, or a forbiddance effect) prevents entry. Halfling marsh-villages have developed the practice of sealing doors and windows with river-clay at night — a tradition whose origin most halflings have forgotten but which remains effective.

**Light Sensitivity (Ex):** An adze in its true form is dazzled in areas of bright sunlight or within the radius of a daylight spell. In firefly form, the adze avoids bright light instinctively but suffers no mechanical penalty — it simply will not enter a well-lit room, which is why adze attacks occur almost exclusively at night.

### Society

Adze have no society. Each is a solitary predator, the remnant of a summoning that no longer has a summoner.

During the Deceivers' War, several Houses — most commonly Aelathar and Lokhlannar — summoned adze from the same plane the Dómnathar host originated from. The adze were used as infiltrators and terror weapons: released into enemy settlements in firefly form, they drained Kin communities of health and morale without the Houses committing a single soldier. An adze planted in a village could weaken an entire population over weeks, softening it for a conventional assault, or possess a community leader and turn them into an unwitting Dómnathar asset.

When the war stalled and the Houses retreated underground, the adze were abandoned. Some were released deliberately — a summoner with no further use for a tool simply breaks the binding and moves on. Others broke free when their summoner was killed in inter-House conflict, the binding unravelling with the caster's death. A few may have been forgotten entirely, left in settlements that the Houses never returned to claim.

The result is a scattering of feral adze across the Deepwater Marches — perhaps a dozen, perhaps fewer, each operating on predatory instinct rather than Dómnathar direction. They haunt halfling river-villages and marsh-trading posts because these settlements offer what an adze needs: sleeping victims in close quarters, warm blood, and darkness. The halflings of the Deepwater Marches have lived with adze predation for so long that they have developed cultural defences without understanding what they are defending against. Sealing homes with river-clay, sleeping under nets treated with palm oil (which adze find repulsive — they will avoid feeding on a creature that smells of palm oil, though the reason is unknown), and keeping lamps burning through the night are all common practices in the deep marsh communities.

No halfling knows that the adze came from the same place as the Deceivers. The connection between the firefly-vampire that drains children in the night and the vast extraplanar threat beneath the world has never been made by surface-dwelling Kin. A scholar or adventurer who discovered this link would fundamentally change how the Deepwater Marches communities understand their oldest and most persistent supernatural threat.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (the planes) can learn the following about adze.

**DC 10:** Adze are malevolent spirits that take the form of fireflies and drain the blood of sleeping victims in the Deepwater Marches. They cause a wasting fever in those they feed upon.

**DC 15:** An adze can slip through any gap that isn't sealed airtight. If caught in firefly form — physically grabbed or trapped — it reverts to a small, hunched humanoid that can be killed by normal means. They can also possess living creatures, changing their host's behaviour over time.

**DC 20:** Adze are outsiders, not native spirits. They are repelled by airtight seals, magical wards against evil, and palm oil (the origin of the halfling practice of oiling sleeping-nets). They produce a void on Kin-sense identical to Dómnathar creatures.

**DC 25:** Adze were originally summoned from the Dómnathar's home plane and used as infiltration weapons during the Deceivers' War. The ones that remain in the Deepwater Marches are feral — their summoners are dead or have abandoned them. They retain their extraplanar nature and could theoretically be banished, though no marsh-village healer has ever thought to try.

# AIR ELEMENTAL

The wind gathers where no wind should be. Dust rises in a spiral, leaves whip into a column, and then it is there — a shape made of rushing air, its outline barely visible except where debris traces its form. It smells of high places: ozone, cold rain, the thin air above the mountaintops. It does not speak, but the sound it makes as it moves is like breathing drawn through a cathedral.

Air elementals are living currents of wind torn from the elemental fabric of Oreka and given purpose. They range from small dust-devils barely taller than a halfling to towering cyclones that darken the sky. In Oreka, they are among the purest expressions of the Youngest Brother's breath — the same divine exhalation that set the world spinning — and their presence is treated with reverence by those cultures closest to the Wind Lord's legacy.

### Combat

Air elementals fight with blinding speed, striking and withdrawing before slower opponents can react. They attack by slamming their bodies of compressed wind into targets — the impact feels like being struck by a wall of solid air, and leaves no visible wound, only shattered ribs and ruptured organs. Against groups, an air elemental uses its whirlwind form to scatter and disorient before picking off isolated targets with slam attacks.

Air elementals exploit three-dimensional space ruthlessly. They hover above melee reach, dive to strike, and withdraw vertically. They are intelligent enough (at higher Hit Dice) to identify spellcasters and prioritize disrupting them with whirlwind. They never willingly fight underground or in enclosed spaces, and an air elemental forced into a confined area retreats immediately if possible.

### Special Abilities

**Air Mastery (Ex):** Airborne creatures take a –1 penalty on attack and damage rolls against an air elemental.

**Whirlwind (Su):** The elemental can transform itself into a whirlwind once every 10 minutes and remain in that form for up to 1 round per 2 HD. In this form, the elemental can move through the space of creatures of a certain size or smaller (see table) and those creatures must succeed on a Reflex save or be caught in the whirlwind. Creatures caught take the listed damage each round and are held suspended, unable to move except by teleportation. They can escape by succeeding on a Reflex save or a DC 20 Strength check as a standard action. The save DC is Strength-based.

**Elemental Resonance (Su):** Air elementals register on Kin-sense as a sharp, bright rush — the feel of wind hitting your face on a high cliff, or the sudden drop in pressure before a thunderstorm. At Small size, the sensation is a faint draft across the back of the neck. At Elder size, Kin within 120 ft. feel their ears pop and their hair lift; the resonance is overwhelming and unmistakable, producing an involuntary urge to look upward. Kin can detect any air elemental within 60 ft. automatically; at Greater or Elder size, the detection range extends to 120 ft. Air elementals are native to Oreka's elemental fabric and produce no void or wrongness — they are the world working as intended.

### Society

Air elementals have no society. They are elemental forces given temporary shape and purpose — summoned, spontaneously generated in places of great wind, or released from the remnants of Giant rune-circles that once maintained atmospheric stability across the Infinite Desert.

Hasura elves consider air elementals sacred and call them *taelbrith* — "wind-thoughts." Hasura druids do not summon them casually and consider binding one to forced service a blasphemy equivalent to slavery. Pasua forest-elves associate them with weather-change and read their spontaneous appearance as omens. Mytroan steppe-riders fear the larger variants, which they call "sky-bulls," and avoid riding during storms in which an elder air elemental might be present.

The Pekakarlik Dwarves of the Infinite Desert have a practical relationship with air elementals: the rune-circles at ancient oases sometimes spontaneously generate small air elementals, and the Pekakarlik allow these to persist as natural ventilation systems in underground settlements. Rarozhki forge-dwarves use bound air elementals to power bellows, which the Hasura consider deeply disrespectful.

### Combat

An allip has no tactics because an allip has no mind left to form them. It drifts toward the nearest living creature and babbles, flooding the area with the broken contents of its shattered psyche. If creatures approach within reach, it attacks with its incorporeal touch, draining Wisdom with each contact — pouring its madness into the victim like water into a cracked vessel. An allip does not pursue fleeing creatures beyond its haunting ground, which is typically the place where it died or the place associated with the knowledge that drove it mad.

An allip cannot be reasoned with, bargained with, or turned aside by any means other than force. It does not recognize other creatures as real.

### Special Abilities

**Babble (Su):** An allip constantly mutters and whimpers to itself, creating a hypnotic effect. All sane creatures within 60 feet of the allip must succeed on a Will save (DC 16) or be fascinated for 2d4 rounds. Creatures that successfully save cannot be affected by the same allip's babble for 24 hours. This is a sonic, mind-affecting compulsion effect. The save DC is Charisma-based.

A creature fascinated by the babble moves toward the allip at its normal speed. If the fascinated creature comes within 5 feet of the allip, it stands still and offers no resistance to the allip's touch, though any attack against the fascinated creature breaks the effect. Fascination also ends if the creature is shaken, dealt damage, or removed from the babble's area.

**Madness (Su):** Anyone targeting an allip with a thought detection, mind control, or telepathic ability makes direct contact with its shattered mind and takes 1d4 points of Wisdom damage.

**Wisdom Drain (Su):** An allip causes 1d4 points of Wisdom drain each time it hits with its incorporeal touch attack. On each such successful attack, the allip gains 5 temporary hit points.

**Undead Resonance (Su):** On Kin-sense, an allip registers as a distorted, agonizing loop — the remnant of a once-living Kin signature, now broken and repeating. Kin describe the sensation as "hearing someone scream a word over and over until it stops sounding like a word." The specific elemental quality of the original signature is still faintly detectable: an allip that was once human carries a ghost of the Youngest Brother's whisper, while one that was once a dwarf echoes the Lady of Fire's heat — but twisted, wrong, recursive. Kin within 30 ft. can detect the allip automatically through Kin-sense and can identify it as undead; the distorted nature of the signal is unmistakable.

### Society

Allips have no society. Each is the isolated remnant of a single person who went irrecoverably mad before dying.

In the Kinsweave, allips are most commonly found in the ruins of the border kingdoms that fell during the Lament of Kings — court scribes who discovered their king's treachery, scholars who decoded forbidden texts, strategists who realized too late that the battle was already lost. Eruskan historians call them *voce perduta* — "lost voices" — and consider their presence in a ruin to be a reliable indicator that the building contains information someone died to learn.

In the Tidebloom Reach, allips haunt the drowned villages near Hillmeet and Foxfen, where flooding during the Reckoning trapped entire communities. These allips babble in dialects that have not been spoken in three hundred years, and Halfling spirit-callers sometimes attempt to listen to the babble for fragments of preserved knowledge — a practice that other Halflings consider suicidally reckless.

Pekakarlik scholars in the Infinite Desert have encountered rare allips in Giant record-halls — not Giant allips (Giants do not rise as undead in the conventional sense; their spirits become Sand Wraiths) but later explorers who went mad attempting to decipher Giant rune-script. These allips sometimes babble fragments of Giant mathematical formulae, which the Pekakarlik transcribe at a safe distance using long-range listening posts.

Their Kin-sense signature is a broken loop of their original elemental identity. Eruskan historians consider their presence an indicator of valuable forgotten knowledge, and Pekakarlik scholars transcribe their babble at a distance for fragments of Giant-era information.

### Combat

An androsphinx is one of the most dangerous combatants in the Infinite Desert, and it knows this. It begins most encounters by speaking — testing the intelligence, purpose, and moral character of those who approach. Creatures that prove hostile, deceitful, or obviously evil receive no further conversation. The sphinx pounces.

In melee, an androsphinx charges with a pounce, delivering a full attack of two claws and two rakes in a single devastating round. Against groups, it uses its roar to weaken or panic enemies before engaging the strongest survivor. An androsphinx fights to the death to protect its territory but will allow honorable opponents to withdraw if they acknowledge defeat. It never pursues fleeing enemies beyond the boundary of the ruin complex it guards.

An androsphinx is intelligent enough to identify spellcasters and prioritize them when outnumbered. It uses Flyby Attack to strafe groups from the air if ground combat is unfavorable.

### Special Abilities

**Pounce (Ex):** If an androsphinx charges a foe, it can make a full attack, including two rake attacks.

**Rake (Ex):** Attack bonus +18 melee, damage 2d4+3. An androsphinx that pounces can make two rake attacks with its hind legs.

**Roar (Su):** An androsphinx can roar up to three times per day. Each successive roar has a different effect, and all three must be used in order. Creatures within range must succeed on the listed save or suffer the effect. The save DCs are Charisma-based.

*First Roar:* All creatures within 60 feet must succeed on a DC 19 Will save or be frightened for 2d6 rounds. This is a sonic, mind-affecting fear effect.

*Second Roar:* All creatures within 60 feet must succeed on a DC 19 Fortitude save or be paralyzed for 1d4 rounds. This is a sonic effect.

*Third Roar:* All creatures within 60 feet must succeed on a DC 19 Fortitude save or take 2d4 points of Strength damage and be stunned for 2d4 rounds. Creatures that succeed take 1d4 Strength damage but are not stunned. Creatures within 30 feet of the sphinx that fail their saves are also deafened for 2d6 rounds. This is a sonic effect. Freestanding stone structures within 100 feet are visibly damaged (hairline cracks, fallen stonework); fragile objects (glass, pottery, thin wooden walls) shatter.

**Elemental Resonance (Su):** An androsphinx registers on Kin-sense as a deep, grounding hum — the Lord of Stone's signature — overlaid with a high, whistling note of the Youngest Brother's wind. The combination is unusual and distinctive: earth and air braided together, as though the ground itself were singing. Kin within 60 ft. can detect the sphinx automatically and sense its dual affinity. The resonance conveys an impression of ancient patience and controlled power — Kin who detect it often describe feeling "steadied," as though the sphinx's presence reinforces their own elemental balance.

**The Harmony (Su):** As a magical beast native to Oreka, the androsphinx is part of the world's elemental fabric. It possesses the Harmony — the species-specific resonance that connects it to all others of its kind. Androsphinxes can sense other sphinxes (andro-, gyno-, crio-, or hieraco-) within 5 miles, though they cannot communicate specific information through this sense, only presence and general emotional state.

### Society

Androsphinxes are solitary by nature, though mated pairs (always with a gynosphinx) are not uncommon in the deeper ruins of the Infinite Desert. They claim territories centered on significant Giant ruins and defend them with absolute dedication — not from possessiveness, but from what appears to be a genuine sense of obligation to preserve the places where the Giants lived.

Pekakarlik Dwarves consider androsphinxes allies and neighbors. Several Pekakarlik oasis-settlements exist in comfortable proximity to sphinx territories, and the dwarves maintain long-standing arrangements: the Pekakarlik maintain the rune-circles and stonework within the ruin, and the sphinx protects the approaches. These relationships can span centuries, with a single sphinx outliving generations of dwarven caretakers. Pekakarlik children are taught to address a sphinx with the formal greeting: "I see you, guardian. I walk with open hands."

Mytroan caravans that cross the desert treat sphinx territories as landmarks and do not camp within them without invitation. Experienced caravan-masters know that an androsphinx will sometimes trade information — directions to water, warnings about sandstorms or Sand Wraith activity — in exchange for news from the wider world.

Taraf-Imro warriors regard the androsphinx with deep respect and consider killing one to be an act so dishonorable it would stain a family for three generations. This is one of very few things that Taraf-Imro and Pekakarlik agree on without argument.

**DC 24:** An androsphinx's roar is its most feared ability — it can frighten, paralyze, or inflict Strength damage depending on which roar it uses. The roars must be used in sequence. Androsphinxes are always chaotic good and will speak before fighting if given the chance.

**DC 29:** In Oreka, androsphinxes claim territories centered on Giant ruins and are considered guardians and allies by the Pekakarlik Dwarves. Some scholars believe androsphinxes predate the Giants' fall and were created by Giant rune-crafting, though no sphinx has ever confirmed or denied this.

### Combat

An ankheg is an ambush predator. It burrows to a depth of 5–10 feet beneath loose soil, using tremorsense to detect prey walking above. When a creature passes within reach, the ankheg erupts from the ground (this does not count as a charge), bites, and attempts to grab the target. A grabbed creature is dragged underground on the ankheg's next turn unless it escapes.

If outmatched or reduced to half hit points, an ankheg spits acid at the most dangerous opponent and retreats underground. It does not fight to the death and abandons its current tunnel network if threatened by a force it cannot overcome, relocating 1d4 miles away to dig new tunnels within a week.

Against multiple opponents, an ankheg grabs the smallest creature and retreats underground with it, relying on the tunnel network to prevent pursuit.

### Special Abilities

**Improved Grab (Ex):** To use this ability, an ankheg must hit with its bite attack. It can then attempt to start a grapple as a free action without provoking an attack of opportunity. If it wins the grapple check, it establishes a hold and can drag the opponent underground on the following round. It automatically deals bite damage each round it maintains the grapple.

**Spit Acid (Ex):** Once every 6 hours, an ankheg can spit a 30-foot line of acid that deals 4d4 acid damage (Reflex DC 14 half). The save DC is Constitution-based. After spitting acid, the ankheg's bite attack deals no acid damage for 6 hours.

**Elemental Affinity (Su):** Ankhegs are earth-attuned creatures — their burrowing is assisted by an instinctive, unconscious resonance with stone and soil. This manifests as a faint vibration in the ground above an ankheg tunnel detectable with a DC 20 Kin-sense check or tremorsense. Ankhegs gain a +2 racial bonus on saves against spells with the Earth descriptor.

**The Harmony (Su):** As an Oreka-native magical beast, the ankheg is part of the Harmony — the species-specific awareness that connects it to other ankhegs. An ankheg can sense the presence of other ankhegs within 1 mile, which prevents tunnel networks from colliding and allows clusters to distribute across productive hunting grounds without competition. This is not communication — it is passive spatial awareness, like the way roots grow away from other roots.

### Society

Ankhegs have no society. They are solitary predators that tolerate one another only during a brief annual mating season (early spring, when the ground thaws).

To Mytroan farmers on the Kinsweave border, ankhegs are the primary agricultural hazard of settled life. An ankheg can devastate a wheat field in a week — not by eating the grain, but by undermining the root structure with its tunnels, causing entire sections to collapse. Mytroan settlements maintain "groundstalkers" — specialists who read the soil for ankheg signs (subtle depressions, unusual crop patterns, the sour smell of acid residue) and organize community hunts.

Warg herders on the open steppe have a more tolerant relationship with ankhegs. Warg camp sites are too temporary for ankhegs to target effectively, and the Wargs have learned that ankheg tunnels — once abandoned — serve as excellent storm shelters. Warg children are taught to test the ground with a spear before stepping off the path, a practice so automatic that adult Wargs do it unconsciously even on stone.

Halflings in the Tidebloom Reach have begun encountering ankhegs only in the last century, as marshland is drained and converted to farmland. The halfling approach is characteristically pragmatic: ankheg chitin, once cleaned of acid residue, makes excellent lightweight armor, and several halfling leatherworkers have developed techniques for curing it. An ankheg carapace in good condition is worth 50–100 gp in Hillmeet.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (arcana) can learn the following about ankhegs.

**DC 13:** Ankhegs are Large burrowing predators that erupt from the ground to snatch prey. They have acidic saliva and strong mandibles.

**DC 18:** An ankheg uses tremorsense to detect prey from underground. It can spit a line of acid once every six hours, but doing so leaves its bite without acid damage for the same period. They prefer to grab and drag victims underground.

**DC 23:** Ankhegs are earth-attuned creatures tied to the Lord of Stone. They are the primary agricultural pest of Mytroan farming communities on the Kinsweave border. Abandoned ankheg tunnels are used as storm shelters by Warg herders. In the Tidebloom Reach, halfling craftspeople have developed techniques for curing ankheg chitin into lightweight armor worth up to 100 gold pieces.

# ANNIS HAG

She is nine feet tall and could be mistaken for a hill giant at a distance — except that hill giants do not move like that, with the hunched, stalking patience of a thing that enjoys the chase more than the kill. Her skin is the blue-black of a deep bruise. Her fingers end in iron-hard nails the color of rust, and when she smiles — and she will smile — her teeth are flat, broad, and set in a jaw that could crack a horse's femur.

### Combat

An annis hag is the most physically dangerous of the hag types and fights accordingly — she does not rely on subtlety when strength will serve. She prefers to ambush lone travelers or small groups, attacking from concealment in rocky terrain or emerging from a cave. She closes to melee immediately, using her reach advantage to engage before opponents expect it.

In grapple, an annis is devastating: she grabs with her claws, rakes with her free hand, and rends if both claws connect. She knows her own toughness and does not retreat easily — she is more likely to finish a fight to the death than flee, unless facing a group that clearly outmatches her. Against a group, she targets the weakest-looking member first, kills them quickly to demoralize the others, and uses the terrain (narrow gullies, cave passages, rockfalls) to prevent being surrounded.

An annis in a covey benefits from the group's cooperative spellcasting and adopts a different role: she is the muscle, absorbing melee attacks while her covey-sisters use their spells from range.

### Special Abilities

**Improved Grab (Ex):** To use this ability, an annis hag must hit a Large or smaller opponent with a claw attack. She can then attempt to start a grapple as a free action without provoking an attack of opportunity.

**Rake (Ex):** Attack bonus +13 melee, damage 1d6+7. An annis hag that successfully grapples a creature can make a rake attack with one of her free claws.

**Rend (Ex):** An annis hag that hits with both claw attacks latches onto the opponent's body and tears the flesh. This attack automatically deals an additional 2d6+10 points of damage.

**Spell-Like Abilities:** At will — *disguise self*, *fog cloud*. Caster level 8th.

**Covey (Su):** When three hags of any type form a covey, they gain access to the following spell-like abilities, usable once each per day by any member of the covey as long as all three are within 10 feet of one another: *animate dead*, *bestow curse* (DC 17), *control weather*, *dream*, *forcecage*, *mind blank*, *mirage arcana* (DC 18), *polymorph*, *veil* (DC 19), *vision*. All abilities function at caster level 9th. The save DCs are based on a Charisma of 16.

**Elemental Resonance (Su):** On Kin-sense, an annis hag registers as something wrong — an earth-signature that reads as stone, but soured, as though the stone had been left in stagnant water. Kin describe it as "the feeling of putting your hand on a cold, damp wall in a place that should be dry." The resonance is not void (she is not Dómnathar) and not distorted (she is not undead) — it is corrupted, as though the earth-affinity itself has curdled. Kin within 30 ft. can detect the annis through Kin-sense but must succeed on a DC 15 Kin-sense check to distinguish her from normal earth-attuned creatures; at DC 20, the corruption becomes obvious.

**The Harmony (Su):** Hags possess a dark version of the Harmony — they can sense other hags within 10 miles, which is how coveys form across vast distances. This sense operates across hag types: an annis can detect a green hag or a sea hag as easily as another annis.

### Society

Annis hags are solitary predators who claim territories in the rocky borderlands between the Eternal Steppe and the mountain ranges — places too rugged for farming, too exposed for permanent settlement, and too far from trade routes for regular patrols. They are the hags that Mytroan steppe-riders fear most, because annis territory overlaps with their grazing routes.

Mytroan culture has an extensive tradition of hag-lore. Children learn the "three signs of the iron crone": bruise-colored skin seen at a distance, the smell of turned earth where no earth has been turned, and the tracks — too large for a woman, too narrow for a giant. Mytroan hunting parties that encounter annis tracks outside established territories organize communal drives to push the hag into open ground, where mounted riders can circle it. These hunts are dangerous and not always successful.

Vestri Dwarves at the Giant's Teeth border have a more clinical relationship with annis hags. The Vestri consider them a known threat, like cave-ins or gas pockets, and deal with them through prepared ambushes rather than mounted hunts. Vestri records note that annis hags occasionally attempt to infiltrate dwarf settlements using *disguise self*, and the Vestri have developed Kin-sense screening protocols at settlement gates specifically because of this threat.

The Wargs avoid annis hags with a fear that goes beyond the practical. Warg oral tradition holds that the first annis hag was a woman who ate her own children during a famine and was cursed by the Lord of Stone — her earth-affinity soured as punishment. Whether this is true or legend, it explains why the annis's Kin-sense signature reads as corrupted earth rather than void or distortion.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (nature) can learn the following about annis hags.

**DC 16:** Annis hags are the largest and most physically powerful hags. They have iron-hard claws, can rend opponents caught in both claws, and have spell resistance.

**DC 21:** An annis hag can disguise herself and create fog to cover her approach. Three hags of any type can form a covey, gaining powerful cooperative spell-like abilities. An annis's damage reduction is overcome by bludgeoning weapons.

**DC 26:** On Kin-sense, an annis hag reads as corrupted earth — soured stone, distinct from both Dómnathar void and undead distortion. Warg tradition holds they were cursed by the Lord of Stone. Hags can sense other hags within ten miles, which is how coveys form across great distances. Vestri Dwarves have developed Kin-sense screening protocols at their gates specifically to detect disguised annis hags.

It looks like a spider — a hand-sized spider, mottled brown and grey, building a web between two branches with the unhurried patience of a craftsman who has all day. ### Combat

Aranea prefer not to fight. They are intelligent, cautious, and aware of their physical fragility — a trait that makes them dangerous, because an aranea that does fight has already decided it can win.

When forced into combat, an aranea begins in spider form, using its web to entangle the most dangerous opponent while casting spells from concealment in the canopy. It favors enchantment and illusion spells that neutralize threats without requiring physical engagement. If combat goes poorly, it flees by climbing, using its 50-ft. speed and canopy knowledge to disappear in rounds.

An aranea colony coordinates through pre-arranged web signals — vibrations transmitted along silk lines connecting their individual territories. A colony under attack can mobilize all members within minutes, and a group of four or more aranea can layer webs and spells to create killing zones that would challenge a party significantly above their individual CR.

An aranea almost never fights in humanoid form. Its natural weapons and web are only available in spider form, and it considers humanoid combat a last resort.

### Special Abilities

**Poison (Ex):** Injury, Fortitude DC 13, initial damage 1d6 Str, secondary damage 2d6 Str. The save DC is Constitution-based.

**Spells:** An aranea casts spells as a 3rd-level sorcerer. It favors enchantment and illusion spells.

*Typical Sorcerer Spells Known (6/6; save DC 12 + spell level):*
*0 — daze, detect magic, ghost sound, resistance; 1st — charm person, sleep, silent image.*

**Web (Ex):** An aranea can throw a web up to six times per day. This is similar to an attack with a net but has a maximum range of 50 feet, with a range increment of 10 feet, and is effective against targets up to one size category larger than the aranea. An entangled creature can escape with a successful Escape Artist check (DC 13) or burst it with a Strength check (DC 17). The check DCs are Constitution-based, with the Strength check including a +4 racial bonus. Each 5-foot section of web has 6 hit points and damage reduction 5/—.

An aranea can also create sheets of sticky webbing, typically in the canopy between trees. Creatures walking into a web must succeed on a DC 20 Spot check to notice it; otherwise they stumble into the web and become entangled as though by a web attack. Attempts to escape or burst the webbing gain a +5 bonus if the creature has something to walk on or grab while pulling free.

**Alternate Form (Su):** An aranea can assume two forms. Each aranea has one specific humanoid form — always Small or Medium — that it retains throughout its life. It also has its natural spider form. Changing form is a standard action. In humanoid form, the aranea retains its ability scores, hit points, and mind but loses its natural attacks, web, and climb speed. It can still cast spells. Equipment worn or carried does not change with it.

**Elemental Resonance (Su):** On Kin-sense, an aranea registers as a dual signature — primarily air (a soft, patient rustle, like wind through silk) with an earthy undertone (the dry warmth of sun-baked wood). In spider form, this signature is subtle and easily overlooked; most Kin would not expect to sense a spider and might not notice the resonance unless specifically concentrating (DC 15 Kin-sense check). In humanoid form, the aranea's resonance reads as a normal — if slightly odd — Kin signature, making it difficult to distinguish from an actual Kin on Kin-sense alone. A DC 20 Kin-sense check reveals something "layered" about the signature, as though the elemental resonance has two textures.

**The Harmony (Su):** As an Oreka-native magical beast, aranea possess the Harmony. They can sense other aranea within 1 mile, which allows colony members to maintain awareness of one another's location and general state. Aranea experience the Harmony as a faint vibration — like a strand of silk being plucked at a distance.

### Society

Aranea are the quiet intelligentsia of the Great Forests — sapient, secretive, and profoundly private. A typical aranea colony of three to six members occupies a section of old-growth canopy and maintains it as a territory, building elaborate web-structures that serve as homes, alarm systems, and art installations simultaneously. Aranea webs are architecturally complex enough that Hasura scholars have studied them for geometric insights.

Hasura elves are the only Kin who maintain regular, acknowledged relations with aranea. The Hasura word for aranea is *selith-kari* — "silk-speaker" — and in several Hasura communities, aranea in humanoid form trade finished textiles, dyed silk, and woven goods at forest markets. These relationships are built on mutual respect for privacy: the Hasura do not visit aranea territories uninvited, and aranea do not use *charm person* on Hasura. Both sides consider this agreement sacred, and violations are punished severely by both communities.

Pasua forest-elves are warier. The Pasua respect aranea intelligence but distrust shapechangers as a category, a prejudice that has deep roots in Pasua encounters with Dómnathar infiltrators during the Deceivers' War. An aranea discovered in humanoid form in Pasua territory faces immediate interrogation — not necessarily hostile, but firm.

Eruskan humans in the Twin Rivers trade network know aranea silk as "forest-weave" and pay premium prices for it. Most Eruskan traders believe the silk is produced by Hasura weavers, and the Hasura see no reason to correct this misunderstanding.

Aranea have no relationship with the Dómnathar and are immune to the False Silence effect — their Kin-sense resonance is natural and cannot be suppressed by Dómnathar methods, which were designed for Kin physiology, not magical beast physiology. An aranea in a community that has been infiltrated by Dómnathar agents might notice something wrong before any Kin does, though it might not know what to call the void-signatures it detects.

### Lore

**DC 14:** Aranea are intelligent, web-spinning magical beasts that can take humanoid form. They are poisonous and can cast arcane spells.

**DC 19:** An aranea has one specific humanoid form it keeps for life. It casts spells as a sorcerer and favors enchantment and illusion. Colonies communicate through web vibrations and can coordinate defenses rapidly.

**DC 24:** Aranea in Oreka are dual-attuned to the Youngest Brother and Lord of Stone. Hasura elves maintain trade relationships with them, and aranea silk — sold as "forest-weave" in Eruskan markets — is worth double standard goods value. Aranea are immune to the Dómnathar's False Silence effect and may detect infiltrators before Kin do, though they do not always know what they are sensing.
