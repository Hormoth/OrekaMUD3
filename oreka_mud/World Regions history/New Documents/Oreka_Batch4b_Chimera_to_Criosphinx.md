# CHIMERA

Three heads. That is the first thing you register — three heads on a body that should not be able to support even one of them. The lion's head roars. The goat's head screams. The dragon's head, dark-scaled and smouldering, simply opens its mouth and breathes fire across the rocks where you were standing a moment ago. The body is massive, a fusion of lion and goat with bat-wings spreading wide enough to blot out the pass behind it. It does not look like something that was designed. It looks like something that was assembled in anger and then let loose.

### Combat

A chimera is chaos with wings. Its three heads operate semi-independently — each reacting to threats from its own direction, each attacking with its own rhythm — which makes the creature devastatingly effective in melee but incapable of coordinated tactics. A chimera does not plan. It charges the nearest enemy, attacks with everything it has, and keeps attacking until the target stops moving or the chimera decides to eat something else.

In combat, a chimera opens with its breath weapon from the air (if flying) or at close range (if on the ground), then closes to full attack — lion bite, goat gore, dragon bite, and claw attacks in a whirlwind of violence. Against multiple opponents, each head tends to fixate on a different target, which means a chimera fighting a group distributes its damage rather than focusing fire. This is a disadvantage against disciplined defenders but an advantage against scattered or panicking enemies.

A chimera's flying speed is poor, and it cannot hover for more than a round without landing. It uses flight primarily for repositioning — swooping to a new angle of attack or escaping to a cliff-perch to use its breath weapon from safety. A chimera that is grounded fights with feral aggression and does not retreat unless reduced to one-quarter hit points.

Chimera prides are rare and terrifying. When multiple chimeras attack, they do not coordinate — they simply attack the same general area simultaneously, creating overlapping kill zones of fire, claws, and trampling.

### Special Abilities

**Breath Weapon (Su):** The dragon head breathes a 20-foot cone of fire, dealing 3d8 fire damage (Reflex DC 17 half). Usable every 1d4 rounds. The save DC is Constitution-based. The fire smells of sulphur and animal musk — a combination that Mytroan scouts find distinctively chimera and can identify at 200 yards downwind.

**Elemental Resonance (Su):** On Kin-sense, a chimera registers as a discordant, layered signature — three elemental threads that do not harmonise. The Fire signature (from the dragon head) dominates, but beneath it are traces of Earth (the lion's predatory solidity) and Air (the goat's high-altitude adaptation and the wings). The resonance feels unstable, like three instruments playing different songs simultaneously. Kin within 60 ft. detect the chimera automatically, and the dissonance is unpleasant — not painful, but grating, like a sound that sets teeth on edge.

Scholars debate whether chimeras are natural creatures or the products of ancient magical experimentation. The elemental dissonance supports the latter theory — no creature born of Oreka's natural elemental fabric should carry three unintegrated affinities. Rarozhki alchemists who have examined chimera remains believe the species was created during the Age of Giants, possibly as guardian beasts that outlived their purpose and went feral.

### Society

Chimeras have no society. They are territorial predators that claim rocky highlands and attack everything that enters their range that looks edible — which, for a chimera, is most things.

Their placement at the steppe-mountain border puts them in direct conflict with both Mytroan steppe-riders and the mountain-dwelling peoples of the Giant's Teeth. Mytroan herding communities consider chimeras the worst aerial threat on the steppe — worse than griffons (which can be driven off) and worse than rocs (which are rare). A chimera that discovers a herd of horses or cattle will return to it nightly until the herd is destroyed or relocated, and its fire breath makes corrals and wooden fencing useless as protection.

Mytroan bands deal with chimeras through organised hunts — a coordinated effort involving mounted archers, fire-resistant hide shields, and the crucial tactic of targeting the goat head first. Mytroan scouts have observed that destroying or severely injuring the goat head disrupts the chimera's coordination; the remaining two heads fight less effectively when the middle head is gone. This technique is passed down in Mytroan hunting-songs.

Taraf-Imro warriors hunt chimeras for sport and glory. A chimera kill is considered a significant achievement — the three-headed beast requires a warrior to fight three opponents simultaneously, which the Taraf-Imro regard as the purest test of combat awareness. Taraf-Imro tradition demands that the hunter take one trophy from each head: a lion's tooth, a goat horn, and a dragon scale, worn together as a triple-kill necklace.

Rarozhki Dwarves in the Scorchspire foothills tolerate chimera prides at a distance because the creatures keep other aerial predators out of their territory. A chimera flight that has claimed a ridge effectively clears it of griffons, hieracosphinxes, and other winged threats, which Rarozhki surface-miners consider a net benefit as long as the chimeras stay on the ridge.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (arcana) can learn the following about chimeras.

**DC 22:** The goat head appears to coordinate the other two — injuring or destroying it reduces the chimera's combat effectiveness. Chimeras are possibly artificial creatures from the Age of Giants, as their elemental signature carries three unintegrated affinities. They will repeatedly attack the same herd until it is destroyed.

**DC 27:** Mytroan hunting doctrine targets the goat head first to disrupt coordination. Taraf-Imro warriors take triple-kill trophies from all three heads. Rarozhki alchemists believe chimeras were created as Giant-era guardian beasts. Their Kin-sense resonance is uniquely discordant — three elemental signatures that never harmonise.

# CHUUL

It rises from the shallows like a nightmare designed by committee — lobster pincers the size of shields, a segmented carapace slick with brine, and a mass of writhing tentacles where its mouth should be. Its eyes, set on short stalks, swivel independently, tracking two different targets at once. It does not look like it belongs in this water. It does not look like it belongs anywhere. It looks like something that crawled out of a place that has different rules about what shapes are acceptable.

### Combat

A chuul hunts from water. It lurks in shallows or tidal pools — its carapace blending with rock and coral — and waits for prey to approach the waterline. When a creature enters reach, the chuul explodes from concealment, grabbing with both pincers and dragging the target into the water.

A chuul's combat sequence is methodical: grab with claws, constrict to immobilise, then transfer the pinned creature to its paralytic tentacles. Once paralysed, the victim is dragged underwater and consumed at the chuul's leisure. Against a group, a chuul grabs one target and retreats into the water with it, relying on depth and darkness to prevent rescue. Multiple chuuls coordinate simple ambushes — one attacks from the front while others approach from the flanks, cutting off retreat from the waterline.

Chuuls are intelligent enough to learn from experience. A chuul that has been driven off by a particular defensive tactic will not fall for it again. Chuuls that have encountered fire-wielding opponents will attack the torch-bearer first. Chuuls that have been hooked by fishermen destroy fishing equipment before attacking the fisher.

### Special Abilities

**Constrict (Ex):** A chuul deals 3d6+5 damage with a successful grapple check.

**Improved Grab (Ex):** To use this ability, a chuul must hit with a claw attack. It can then attempt to start a grapple as a free action without provoking an attack of opportunity. If it wins the grapple check, it establishes a hold and can constrict.

**Paralytic Tentacles (Ex):** A chuul can transfer a grappled creature of Large or smaller size from a claw to its tentacles as a move action. The tentacles grapple with the same strength as the claw. A creature grappled by tentacles must succeed on a DC 19 Fortitude save each round or be paralysed for 6 rounds. The save DC is Constitution-based. While paralysed, the creature can take no actions and is helpless. The paralysis is a toxin — the tentacles exude a neurotoxic mucus that penetrates skin on contact.

**Breach Resonance (Su):** Chuuls are not native to Oreka. They are aberrations — creatures from an alien ecology that entered through the Silence Breach or through older, smaller planar tears. On Kin-sense, a chuul registers as wrong — not void (like the Dómnathar), not distorted (like undead), not static (like goblinoids), but a signal that does not correspond to any category in the Kin-sense framework. Kin describe it as "a chord in a key that doesn't exist" or "the feeling of hearing a language that uses sounds your mouth cannot make."

The wrongness is not aggressive — it does not cause pain or fear — but it is deeply unsettling and unmistakable. Kin within 60 ft. detect a chuul automatically and know immediately that whatever they are sensing is not from Oreka. This makes chuuls easy to detect but psychologically disturbing to encounter, particularly for Kin who have never felt a breach-resonance before.

### Society

Chuuls have no culture, no language, and no social structure beyond the pack. They are intelligent predators operating on alien instincts in a world they did not evolve for.

The chuul population in Oreka is concentrated along the Apelian Sea coast, where the deep water and extensive tidal cave systems provide ideal habitat. Smaller populations exist in the Tidebloom Reach, particularly in the flooded Giant ruins at Hillmeet and Foxfen — two devastated settlements from the Deceivers' War where rising water levels have created an extensive network of submerged chambers.

How chuuls arrived in Oreka is debated. The most widely accepted theory holds that they entered through the Silence Breach along with the Dómnathar host — not as members of the army, but as creatures from the same alien ecology that were swept through the planar tear and deposited in Oreka's waters. The Dómnathar do not control chuuls and have never claimed them as servitor species. Alternative theories suggest chuuls predate the Breach entirely, having entered through smaller, older planar tears that existed in the deep Apelian basin before the Deceivers' War.

Halfling fishing communities along the Deepwater Marches coast encounter chuuls regularly and fear them deeply. A chuul in a fishing ground can destroy a community's livelihood in weeks — the creatures consume fish stocks, attack fishing boats, and prey on fishers at the waterline. Halfling coastal villages maintain "chuul watches" during the warm months (when chuuls are most active), with sentries monitoring the shallows for the telltale breach-resonance that signals a chuul's presence.

Na'wasua sea-elves are the most experienced chuul fighters in Oreka. Na'wasua divers encounter chuuls in the deeper Apelian waters and have developed specific combat techniques: attacking from below (where the chuul's ventral carapace is thinnest), targeting the tentacle-mass with slashing weapons to sever the paralytic appendages, and using coordinated teams to separate a chuul from its prey. Na'wasua consider chuuls abominations — alien intrusions into the Lady of the Sea's domain — and kill them on sight.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (dungeoneering) can learn the following about chuuls.

**DC 22:** A chuul transfers grappled victims to its tentacles, which exude a paralytic toxin. They are immune to poison. Chuuls are not native to Oreka — they are alien creatures whose Kin-sense signature registers as fundamentally wrong.

**DC 27:** Chuuls probably entered Oreka through the Silence Breach, though some scholars believe they predate the Deceivers' War. Na'wasua sea-elves have developed specific anti-chuul techniques targeting the ventral carapace and tentacle-mass. Halfling fishing communities maintain warm-season chuul watches, and the flooded Giant ruins at Hillmeet and Foxfen harbour significant chuul populations.

### Combat

A cloaker is an ambush predator that fights from concealment. It hangs from cavern ceilings, flattening its body against the stone until it resembles a natural rock formation or hanging leather — its Hide bonus in dim or dark environments is sufficient to fool most observers. When prey passes beneath, it drops and engulfs.

A cloaker's primary tactic is engulf: it wraps its body around a victim, biting with its inner mouth while using its tough hide to shield itself from the victim's allies. Striking an engulfing cloaker risks hitting the creature it has wrapped around, which forces attackers to aim carefully or accept the collateral damage. While engulfing, the cloaker uses its moan to disable other nearby creatures, preventing rescue.

If outnumbered or facing a strong party, a cloaker uses shadow shift to create illusory duplicates of itself, sowing confusion and buying time to engulf its primary target. A cloaker that is badly wounded (one-quarter hit points) releases its victim and flees upward into the cavern ceiling, using its fly speed and the darkness of the upper cavern to escape.

Cloakers are intelligent. They observe parties from concealment before attacking, identify the most vulnerable target (typically the smallest or least-armoured), and time their drop for maximum surprise. A mob of cloakers coordinates ambushes — two drop simultaneously on different targets while a third uses moan to disable the rest of the group.

### Special Abilities

**Engulf (Ex):** A cloaker can engulf a Medium or smaller creature by dropping onto it from above or by making a successful grapple check. An engulfed creature is pinned and takes 1d6+5 bludgeoning damage plus 1d4+2 bite damage per round. Attacks against the cloaker while it is engulfing deal half their damage to the engulfed creature. An engulfed creature can attempt to escape with a grapple check or an Escape Artist check (DC 17).

**Moan (Ex):** A cloaker can produce a subsonic moan as a standard action. All creatures within 60 feet must save or suffer one of the following effects (determined by the cloaker): Unnerve (Will DC 15, –2 penalty on attack and damage rolls), Fear (Will DC 15, panicked for 2 rounds), Nausea (Fortitude DC 15, nauseated for 1d4+1 rounds), or Stupor (Will DC 15, creature can take only a single move action per round for 1d4 rounds). The save DCs are Charisma-based. A cloaker can change the moan's effect each round as a free action. This is a sonic, mind-affecting ability.

**Shadow Shift (Su):** Three times per day, a cloaker can manipulate shadows to create one of the following effects: Obscure Self (as blur, CL 6th), Shadow Images (as mirror image, CL 6th, creating 1d4+2 duplicates), or Silent Image (as the spell, CL 6th, DC 15). These effects last 6 rounds or until dismissed.

**Breach Resonance (Su):** Like chuuls, cloakers are alien to Oreka's elemental framework. On Kin-sense, they register as fundamentally wrong — a signal that belongs to no known category. However, a cloaker's breach-resonance is fainter and harder to detect than a chuul's, partly because the creature actively suppresses its own signature while hunting. A cloaker hanging motionless on a cavern ceiling requires a DC 22 Kin-sense check to detect. A cloaker in motion or actively moaning is easier to detect (DC 14), as the moan resonates through the Kin-sense field as well as through the air.

### Society

Cloakers are intelligent, solitary predators of the deep underground. They have a rudimentary language (a system of subsonic moans and body-posture signals) and can communicate with each other over distances of several hundred feet through stone, using the same subsonic frequencies as their moan attack.

Cloakers in Oreka are concentrated in two types of environment: natural deep cavern systems (where they are native apex predators) and the fringes of Dómnathar tunnel networks (where they are scavengers and opportunists). The Dómnathar tolerate cloakers in the outer reaches of their tunnels for the same reason they tolerate black puddings — the creatures serve as passive security, ambushing intruders before they reach Deceiver strongholds.

Vestri Dwarves encounter cloakers in deep mining operations and regard them as one of the most dangerous tunnel hazards after Dómnathar agents and black puddings. Vestri mining protocol requires all deep-tunnel teams to include a "roof-watcher" — a team member whose sole responsibility is to scan the ceiling with a hooded lantern before the group advances. Vestri roof-watchers are trained to look for the tell-tale signs: a patch of ceiling that seems too smooth, a shadow that doesn't move with the lantern, or a faint subsonic hum at the edge of hearing.

Pekakarlik Dwarves who maintain the Giant ruin undercrofts beneath the Infinite Desert encounter cloakers less frequently but in larger groups — the extensive, interconnected tunnel systems beneath the desert support cloaker mobs that are rare in smaller cavern networks.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (dungeoneering) can learn the following about cloakers.

**DC 15:** Cloakers are intelligent, manta-like creatures that hang from cavern ceilings and drop onto prey. They engulf victims in their bodies and produce a subsonic moan that induces fear, nausea, or stupor.

**DC 20:** Striking a cloaker while it is engulfing a victim deals half the damage to the victim. Cloakers can create illusory duplicates of themselves using shadow manipulation. They are intelligent enough to identify the weakest member of a group before attacking.

**DC 25:** Cloakers are alien aberrations whose Kin-sense signature registers as wrong but is actively suppressed during hunting, making motionless cloakers nearly undetectable. Vestri Dwarves use dedicated roof-watchers in deep tunnel teams. The Dómnathar tolerate cloakers in their tunnel fringes as passive security against intruders.

### Combat

Cloud giants fight with the measured confidence of creatures that have been apex beings for longer than most civilizations have existed. They do not rush, do not panic, and do not waste effort. A cloud giant opens combat by throwing rocks from its summit position — its range increment of 140 feet and its vantage point mean that approaching enemies are under bombardment long before they reach melee range.

In close combat, a cloud giant wields its morningstar with devastating power, using Awesome Blow and Improved Bull Rush to hurl enemies off cliff-edges, out of passes, or simply far enough away to resume rock throwing. Against flying opponents, a cloud giant uses its spell-like abilities (fog cloud, levitate, obscuring mist) to negate aerial advantages and force the engagement onto its terms.

Cloud giants prefer to avoid lethal combat with Kin. If attacked by a Kin party, a cloud giant will attempt to drive the attackers away rather than kill them — using intimidation, rock throws aimed at the ground near (not at) the intruders, and fog to obscure the approach. Only if the party persists past multiple warnings does a cloud giant fight to kill. Against Dómnathar, no warnings are given.

### Special Abilities

**Rock Throwing (Ex):** Range increment 140 ft. A cloud giant can hurl rocks of 60–80 pounds.

**Rock Catching (Ex):** A cloud giant can catch Small, Medium, or Large rocks (or projectiles of similar shape). A successful Reflex save (DC 15 + damage dice) allows the giant to catch the projectile.

**Oversized Weapons (Ex):** A cloud giant wields weapons one size category larger than its size (Gargantuan) without penalty.

**Spell-Like Abilities:** At will — fog cloud, levitate, obscuring mist; 1/day — control weather. Caster level 17th. These abilities reflect the cloud giant's profound connection to the Youngest Brother's domain.

**Giant Remnant Resonance (Su):** This is the defining Kin-sense characteristic of all true Giants. On Kin-sense, a cloud giant registers as all four elemental signatures in harmony — Earth, Fire, Water, and Air — simultaneously, balanced, and overwhelmingly powerful. The experience of detecting a Giant's resonance is unlike anything else in Oreka. Kin within 120 ft. feel the full-element harmony as an involuntary emotional response: awe, reverence, grief, or joy, depending on the individual Kin's temperament and cultural background.

For most Kin, detecting a Giant's resonance is the closest they will ever come to feeling what Oreka's elemental fabric sounded like when it was whole — before the Elemental Lords vanished, before the Giants faded, before the Shattering broke the world into fragments. The resonance carries a weight of time and loss that scholars describe as "hearing the first chord of a song that stopped being sung before you were born."

The Air signature dominates in cloud giants specifically, but all four elements are present and in balance. This balance is what distinguishes Giant resonance from, say, a cherufe's fire-earth fusion or a ccoa's air-earth duality — the Giants' resonance is not a combination but a unity.

### Society

Cloud giants are the last true Giants in Oreka — and they are barely that. The Firstborn, the original Giants shaped by all four Elemental Lords together, are gone. They faded during the Thalorien — the "stone's sleep" — crumbling to dust over millennia as their immortal bodies finally lost coherence. What remain are their descendants: smaller, shorter-lived, less powerful, but still carrying the unmistakable four-element resonance of the Firstborn in their blood.

Cloud giants are the most isolated of the remnant Giant types. They lair on the highest peaks of the Giant's Teeth, above the permanent snowline, in structures carved from the living stone of the mountain — chairs, platforms, shelters, and halls that are not quite architecture and not quite natural formation but something between. These structures are not Giant ruins; they are Giant homes, still occupied, still maintained by the hands that built them.

The cloud giant population in Oreka is small and declining. Current estimates (based on Vestri Dwarf observation and Orean human reports) place the total number of cloud giants in the Giant's Teeth at fewer than forty individuals. They do not reproduce frequently — a mated pair may produce one child per century — and their numbers have been falling since the Thalorien began.

Cloud giants remember. This is their defining cultural characteristic and their greatest burden. They remember the Firstborn, the Age of Giants, the Thalorien, and the Shattering. Their oral tradition — transmitted through enormous, slow-paced songs that can last for days — preserves knowledge of the world as it was before the Kin were shaped. A cloud giant who consents to speak with a Kin scholar can provide information about the Age of Giants that no other source in Oreka can confirm or deny.

Vestri Dwarves maintain formal diplomatic relationships with individual cloud giants, visiting specific summits at appointed intervals (typically once per decade) to exchange news, trade goods (gemstones and worked metal for Giant-era knowledge), and renew non-aggression agreements. The Vestri consider these relationships among their most valuable diplomatic assets and guard the details jealously.

Orean humans in the Giant's Teeth live in the cloud giants' shadow — sometimes literally — and have developed a culture of deference that borders on worship. Orean communities do not climb above the snowline without invitation. When a cloud giant descends to the lower peaks (which happens rarely, usually during severe weather or when the giant needs something it cannot obtain at altitude), the nearest Orean settlement treats the event as a visitation of sacred significance.

Pekakarlik Dwarves know of cloud giants but rarely interact with them. Pekakarlik traders who cross the Giant's Teeth give the highest peaks a wide berth and leave offerings (gemstones, worked copper) at designated cairns along the high passes — tribute to the Giants that the Pekakarlik have paid for as long as their culture has existed, and which they would consider shameful to discontinue.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (nature) can learn the following about cloud giants.

**DC 16:** Cloud giants are enormous, air-attuned beings that dwell on the highest mountain peaks. They can control weather, throw boulders with devastating accuracy, and prefer to drive intruders away rather than kill them.

**DC 21:** Cloud giants are the last remnants of the original Giant race. Their Kin-sense resonance carries all four elemental signatures in harmony — an experience that provokes involuntary emotional responses in detecting Kin. Fewer than forty are believed to exist in Oreka.

**DC 26:** Cloud giants remember the Age of Giants through oral tradition preserved in days-long songs. Vestri Dwarves maintain once-per-decade diplomatic visits to specific individuals. Orean humans treat cloud giant descents as sacred events. Their population is declining due to extremely low reproduction rates, and some Kin scholars fear the cloud giants will follow the Firstborn into extinction within the next millennium.

# COCKATRICE

It struts out of the tall grass like it owns the steppe — a rooster-sized creature with the body of a scrawny chicken, the wings of a bat, and a tail that ends in a scaly, serpentine point. Its feathers are patchy and iridescent, shifting between sickly green and tarnished bronze. Its eyes are beady, stupid, and mean. It looks ridiculous. Then it pecks a field-mouse and the mouse turns to stone mid-squeak, and you stop laughing.

### Combat

A cockatrice is aggressive, territorial, and too stupid to be afraid. It attacks creatures many times its size without hesitation, pecking and flapping and generally behaving like the world's most dangerous chicken. Its combat strategy, such as it is, consists of running at the nearest creature and biting it until the creature turns to stone.

A cockatrice flock is a serious hazard. Multiple cockatrices attacking simultaneously force multiple petrification saves per round, and their small size and erratic movement patterns make them difficult to target. Flocks do not coordinate — each cockatrice attacks independently and usually fixates on a different target — but the sheer volume of petrifying bites creates a dangerous situation for any group caught in the open.

Cockatrices cannot sustain flight for long periods; their poor flying speed and small wings mean they tire quickly. They fly primarily to escape melee or to close distance for an attack, landing after one or two rounds.

### Special Abilities

**Petrification (Su):** A creature hit by a cockatrice's bite must succeed on a DC 12 Fortitude save or be permanently turned to stone. The save DC is Constitution-based. The petrification is instantaneous — there is no gradual onset as with basilisk petrification. One moment the creature is alive; the next, it is a statue. Stone to flesh reverses the effect.

A cockatrice is immune to the petrification of other cockatrices but not to basilisk petrification or other sources.

**Elemental Resonance (Su):** On Kin-sense, a cockatrice registers as a small, sharp earth-signature — a chip of the Lord of Stone's resonance compressed into an aggressively compact package. The resonance is disproportionately strong for the creature's size; Kin within 30 ft. feel a brief, stabbing pulse of earth-energy, like stepping on a sharp stone. Mytroan herders who live in cockatrice territory learn to recognise this pulse and respond by moving their herds away from the area.

**The Harmony (Su):** Cockatrices possess the Harmony and sense other cockatrices within 1 mile. Flocks use this awareness to avoid overlapping territories during nesting season and to congregate at productive hunting grounds.

### Society

Cockatrices are aggressive, stupid animals. They have no culture, no language, and no behavior more complex than eat, mate, and attack things that move.

Their ecological significance in Oreka is primarily as an agricultural hazard. On the Eternal Steppe, where Mytroan herding communities maintain large herds of horses, cattle, and sheep, a cockatrice flock that wanders into grazing land can petrify livestock at a devastating rate. Mytroan herders consider cockatrices the most annoying creature on the steppe — not the most dangerous (that honour goes to the bulette or the annis hag), but the most persistently, irritatingly destructive to the pastoral economy.

Mytroan pest-control methods for cockatrices are well-developed. The preferred technique is the "stone-circle drive": herders on horseback encircle a cockatrice flock and drive it toward a designated killing ground — a rocky area where the flock is surrounded and dispatched with ranged weapons from horseback. The key is never to approach on foot, because a cockatrice in the grass can bite an ankle before the victim sees it coming. Mytroan children are taught to walk through tall steppe-grass by stamping their feet — the vibration drives cockatrices away.

Pekakarlik alchemists purchase cockatrice feathers and blood, which have applications in petrification-related research and in the crafting of stone to flesh scrolls. A fresh cockatrice carcass is worth 50 gp to a Pekakarlik alchemist, which has created a small but persistent trade in cockatrice hunting along the desert-steppe border.

Warg communities on the steppe tolerate cockatrices because the Wargs' thick fur and tough hide provide some protection against the creature's weak bite. Warg pups occasionally play with cockatrices in a way that horrifies Kin observers — batting the creatures around like toys, apparently unaffected by the petrification (Wargs have a +4 racial bonus on Fortitude saves against petrification, a fact that Kin scholars find both interesting and unexplained).

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (arcana) can learn the following about cockatrices.

**DC 13:** Cockatrices are small, chicken-like creatures whose bite turns victims to stone instantly. They are common on the Eternal Steppe and extremely aggressive despite their small size.

**DC 18:** A cockatrice flock is far more dangerous than a solitary specimen because multiple petrification saves are required each round. They cannot sustain flight for long. Stone to flesh reverses the petrification.

**DC 23:** Cockatrices are earth-attuned, like basilisks — their petrification is an expression of the Lord of Stone's elemental domain. Mytroan herders use horseback drives to clear flocks from grazing land. Wargs appear resistant to cockatrice petrification for unknown reasons. Pekakarlik alchemists buy cockatrice remains for petrification-related research.

# CRIOSPHINX

The creature on the ridgeline has the body of a lion — massive, tawny, heavy with muscle — and the head of a ram, curling horns spiralling out from a skull broader than a man's chest. Its wings are folded against its flanks, eagle-feathered and enormous. It watches you approach with an expression that is neither hostile nor welcoming but evaluative, as though it is calculating something about you that you are not aware of.

### Combat

A criosphinx is a brute-force predator — less intelligent and less magical than its androsphinx and gynosphinx cousins, but physically more aggressive. It charges. That is its primary tactic and the one it is best at: a criosphinx's powerful charge delivers a devastating gore attack that can shatter shields, break ribs, and send opponents flying.

In combat, a criosphinx opens with a charge from elevation — using its wings to gain speed on a downhill run — and delivers a powerful charge gore to the largest or most heavily-armoured target. After the initial impact, it pounces, following up with claw attacks against the staggered opponent. If the first charge does not end the fight, the criosphinx withdraws to a distance, wheels, and charges again. This pattern — charge, strike, withdraw, charge — continues until the opponent is dead, crippled, or has fled.

A criosphinx does not use its flying ability in combat; its poor flying speed makes it vulnerable to ranged attacks while airborne. It flies only to reposition between charges or to escape.

Against sphinx-hunting parties, a criosphinx fights with patient aggression, using the terrain of the Scorchspire foothills to force attackers into narrow passes where its charge is most effective and their numbers count for least.

### Special Abilities

**Powerful Charge (Ex):** A criosphinx that charges deals 4d6+12 damage with its gore attack (instead of the normal 2d6+6). This devastating impact can knock opponents off their feet — in addition to the damage, the target must succeed on a DC 21 Fortitude save or be knocked prone. The save DC is Strength-based.

**Pounce (Ex):** If a criosphinx charges, it can make a full attack (including claw attacks) at the end of the charge.

**Elemental Resonance (Su):** On Kin-sense, a criosphinx registers as a dense, hot dual-signature — the Lady of Fire's heat compressed into the Lord of Stone's weight, like a forge-stone that has been heated until it glows. The resonance is aggressive but not hostile; Kin describe it as "the feeling that something very heavy and very warm is paying attention to you." Within 60 ft., the resonance is unmistakable and impossible to misidentify — the sphinx-signature is unique in Oreka's bestiary.

The criosphinx's resonance is notably less complex than an androsphinx's or gynosphinx's. Where the greater sphinxes carry layered, nuanced signatures that communicate mood and intent, the criosphinx's resonance is blunt and straightforward — heat, weight, attention. This simplicity reflects the criosphinx's nature: it is not a guardian, a riddler, or a sage. It is a territorial predator with a sphinx's body and a ram's temperament.

**The Harmony (Su):** Criosphinxes possess the Harmony and can sense other sphinxes (of any type) within 5 miles. This awareness maintains territorial separation between sphinx species. Criosphinxes avoid androsphinxes entirely (the androsphinx is larger, stronger, and magically superior) and maintain wary distance from gynosphinxes. Two criosphinxes whose territories overlap will fight, and the loser departs.

### Society

Criosphinxes are solitary territorial predators. They claim ridgelines and plateau-tops in the Scorchspire foothills and defend them against all comers — including other criosphinxes, which they fight for territory during the annual rut.

The criosphinx occupies a specific ecological niche among Oreka's sphinxes: it is the foothills predator, the sphinx of the lower slopes, while androsphinxes claim the high desert and gynosphinxes claim the Glass Wastes. This territorial stratification is maintained by the Harmony — each sphinx type senses the others and adjusts its territory accordingly.

Rarozhki Dwarves encounter criosphinxes at the lower slopes of the Scorchspires and have a pragmatic relationship with them. A criosphinx that has claimed a ridge near a Rarozhki settlement is a nuisance (it occasionally kills livestock and threatens lone travelers) but also a deterrent — other predators avoid criosphinx territory, and a ridgeline claimed by a sphinx is a ridgeline free of chimeras, wyverns, and hieracosphinxes. Rarozhki communities near active criosphinx territories post warning markers at the boundary and advise travelers to avoid the ridge.

Taraf-Imro warriors consider criosphinx horns among the most prestigious trophies available in the Scorchspire foothills. A criosphinx horn — massive, spiralling, and dense as stone — is worth 500 gp to a Taraf-Imro trophy-trader and considerably more as a crafting material. Taraf-Imro horn-hunts are dangerous, carefully planned affairs involving teams of warriors who use the charge-and-withdraw pattern against the sphinx itself — meeting the creature's charge with a coordinated sidestep and striking the flanks as it passes. Casualties are expected and accepted.

Androsphinxes regard criosphinxes with mild contempt — the way a scholar might regard a particularly aggressive dog. When an androsphinx and a criosphinx's territories intersect, the criosphinx defers immediately. This hierarchy is absolute and has never been challenged.

### Lore

Characters with ranks in Knowledge (arcana) can learn the following about criosphinxes.

**DC 17:** Criosphinxes are ram-headed sphinxes found in the Scorchspire foothills. They are aggressive territorial predators that charge with devastating force. They are less intelligent and less magical than other sphinx types.

**DC 22:** A criosphinx's charge deals massive damage and can knock opponents prone. They maintain territorial separation from other sphinx species through the Harmony. Rarozhki communities tolerate nearby criosphinxes because they deter other predators.

**DC 27:** Criosphinx resonance is fire-earth dual-attuned but simpler than other sphinxes' signatures — blunt heat and weight rather than layered complexity. Taraf-Imro warriors hunt them for their horns, which are valuable as trophies and crafting materials. Criosphinxes defer immediately to androsphinxes in all territorial disputes.
